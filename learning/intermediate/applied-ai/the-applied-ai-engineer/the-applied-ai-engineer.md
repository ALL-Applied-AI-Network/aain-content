# The Applied AI Engineer

The biggest career opportunity in AI right now isn't building foundation models — it's helping real businesses implement them. This is the role of the **applied AI engineer**.

## The Implementation Gap

Most companies know AI matters. Far fewer have actually deployed it in production. The gap between "we should use AI" and "AI is generating value in our workflows" is massive, and it's where applied AI engineers live.

This isn't about training GPT-5. It's about understanding what tools exist today, how to wire them into real systems, and how to deliver measurable value to an organization.

## What Makes This Different from Traditional ML Engineering

Traditional ML roles focus on model architecture, training pipelines, and research. Applied AI engineering focuses on:

- **Integration** — Connecting AI APIs and models to existing business systems
- **Prompt engineering** — Getting reliable, structured outputs from language models
- **Tool-building** — Creating internal tools, agents, and workflows that multiply what people can do
- **Deployment** — Getting AI from a notebook into production where it serves real users
- **Domain knowledge** — Understanding the business problem well enough to know which AI approach actually helps

You don't need a PhD. You need to be able to ship things that work.

## The Career Opportunity

The demand for people who can implement AI is growing faster than the supply. Companies that have billions in AI budget are struggling to find people who can turn that spend into working products.

This is especially true at the intersection of AI and industry-specific knowledge. A student who understands both supply chain operations *and* how to build an RAG pipeline is worth more than someone who only knows one side.
