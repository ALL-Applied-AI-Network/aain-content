# Building a Recurring Series

Running one great talk is an event. Running ten great talks is a brand. A recurring speaker series gives your club a reliable heartbeat, builds a reputation with faculty and industry, and creates a content library that compounds in value over time.

## Why Go Recurring

- **Consistency builds habits.** Members start blocking the time on their calendars. Attendance becomes automatic.
- **Speakers come to you.** Once your series has a track record, people ask to speak instead of you chasing them.
- **Content accumulates.** After one semester, you have 6-8 recorded talks that you can use for promotion, onboarding, and credibility.
- **Sponsors pay attention.** A series with predictable attendance is easier to sponsor than one-off events.

## Choosing a Cadence

| Cadence | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|---------|------|------|----------|
| Weekly | Maximum momentum | Hard to sustain, speaker burnout, organizer burnout | Large clubs with deep speaker pipelines |
| Bi-weekly | Sustainable, predictable | Still requires consistent speaker sourcing | Most clubs (recommended) |
| Monthly | Low effort, easy to maintain quality | Harder to build momentum, members forget | Small clubs or clubs running other event types in between |

**Recommendation:** Bi-weekly is the sweet spot for most clubs. Run your speaker series every other week and use the alternate weeks for workshops, project nights, or social events. This keeps the calendar full without burning out your team.

### Picking Your Day and Time

- **Best days:** Tuesday, Wednesday, or Thursday evenings (Monday is too early in the week, Friday is a lost cause)
- **Best times:** 5:30-7:00 PM or 6:00-7:30 PM (after classes, before people go home)
- **Consistency matters more than optimization.** Pick a slot and stick with it all semester. "Every other Thursday at 6 PM" is a brand.
- **Check for conflicts:** Avoid clashing with popular classes, other club meetings, or recurring campus events.

## Semester Planning

### The Semester Arc Approach

Plan your series like a season of content with an intentional arc:

**Example: "AI in Industry" Semester Arc**
| Week | Speaker | Topic |
|------|---------|-------|
| 2 | Local startup founder | How we use ML to solve [problem] |
| 4 | Alumni (2 years out) | From student project to production ML pipeline |
| 6 | Industry panel (3 speakers) | Careers in AI: what companies actually look for |
| 8 | Research professor | Cutting-edge: what is coming in the next 2 years |
| 10 | Senior engineer at large company | Scaling ML systems from prototype to millions of users |
| 12 | Club showcase + guest judge | Members present their semester projects |

**Why arcs work:** They create narrative momentum. Members feel like they are going somewhere, not just attending random talks.

### The Variety Approach

Alternatively, mix topics and formats for variety:

- Week 2: Technical deep dive (single speaker)
- Week 4: Career panel (3-4 panelists)
- Week 6: Fireside chat with a founder
- Week 8: Lightning talks (4-5 club members)
- Week 10: Technical deep dive (single speaker)
- Week 12: End-of-semester AMA with industry guest

Both approaches work. Choose based on your speaker availability and audience preferences.

## Building a Speaker Pipeline

The number one reason recurring series fail is running out of speakers. Build a pipeline so you are never scrambling.

### The Pipeline Spreadsheet

Maintain a running list with these columns:

| Column | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| Name | Speaker's full name |
| Role / Company | Their current position |
| Topic | What they could talk about |
| Contact | Email, LinkedIn |
| Source | How you found them (referral, LinkedIn, conference, etc.) |
| Status | Not contacted / Contacted / Confirmed / Completed / Declined |
| Date | Scheduled or completed date |
| Notes | Any relevant context |

### Keeping the Pipeline Full

- After every talk, ask the speaker for 1-2 referrals (see [Post-Event Follow-Up](./post-event.md))
- At every club meeting, ask members: "Who would you love to hear from?"
- Follow AI/ML content creators and conference speakers -- add interesting ones to your list
- Build relationships with 2-3 companies that can provide speakers regularly
- Coordinate with other ALL Applied AI Network hubs to share speaker contacts
- Aim to always have the next 3 talks confirmed and 5+ speakers in your pipeline

### The "Never Scrambling" Rule

At any point during the semester, you should have:
- This week's speaker confirmed and prepped
- Next talk's speaker confirmed
- The talk after that at least in email outreach
- 5+ names in the pipeline for future talks

If you fall below this, spend a focused hour doing outreach to rebuild the buffer.

## Creating a Brand Identity

A branded series is more shareable, more memorable, and easier to promote than generic "club talk" events.

### Naming Your Series

Pick a name that is:
- Short and memorable
- Connected to your club identity
- Easy to hashtag

**Examples:**
- "AI Unpacked" (weekly deep dives)
- "Build with AI" (practitioner stories)
- "The Applied AI Speaker Series" (straightforward, professional)
- "[Club Name] Talks" (simple, scalable)

### Visual Identity

- Create a consistent template for promotional graphics (Canva is free and sufficient)
- Use the same colors, fonts, and layout for every talk announcement
- Include your series name, speaker photo, topic, and date on every graphic
- This consistency builds recognition -- people start to recognize your posts at a glance

### Consistent Format

- Same opening structure every time (intro, speaker, Q&A, closing)
- Same time slot every talk
- Same promotional cadence (see [Promotion and Attendance](./promotion.md))
- Same follow-up process (see [Post-Event Follow-Up](./post-event.md))

Consistency reduces organizer effort (you are running a system, not reinventing the wheel) and sets clear expectations for attendees and speakers.

## Measuring Success

Track these metrics each semester:

| Metric | How to Track | Target |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Average attendance | Headcount at each event | Growing or stable semester over semester |
| Unique attendees | Sign-in sheets or check-in data | 2-3x your average attendance (means different people come to different talks) |
| Speaker satisfaction | Post-event email feedback | Speakers willing to refer others |
| Recording views | YouTube/platform analytics | Growing over time |
| New member conversion | Track who joins the club after attending a talk | 10-20% of first-time attendees |

## Scaling Your Series

As your series grows, consider:

- **Co-hosting with other departments or clubs** to reach new audiences
- **Streaming on YouTube or Twitch** to build an online audience beyond your campus
- **Inviting speakers from other ALL Applied AI Network hubs** for cross-campus virtual talks
- **Creating a podcast** from your recorded talks (minimal editing required)
- **Sponsoring the series** (a company sponsors an entire semester of talks in exchange for branding and recruiting access)

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Starting too ambitious.** Do not commit to weekly if you have never done bi-weekly. Scale up after a successful semester.
2. **Inconsistent scheduling.** Skipping weeks or changing the time slot destroys the habit. If you need to skip, announce it and give the reason.
3. **Relying on one person.** The speaker series lead should not be the only person who can run an event. Cross-train at least 2 other officers.
4. **Ignoring feedback.** If attendance drops, ask your members why. The answer is usually "the topics aren't relevant to me" or "the time doesn't work."
5. **Not recording.** Every talk you do not record is content you cannot use later. Record everything (with speaker consent).
