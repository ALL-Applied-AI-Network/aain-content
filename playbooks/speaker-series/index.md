# Speaker Series

Bi-weekly Thursdays in the ITC Great Hall. Free food, a smart person from industry, and a room of students who care.

The speaker series is the lowest-effort, highest-leverage event format we run. In 2024 alone MAIC hosted 10+ industry speakers including teams from NVIDIA, Direct Supply, Kohl's, and Generac. 40–50 members attend each one. Our best-attended was an NVIDIA Q&A panel in 2024 with 80+ in a room that seats 120. Our worst was a 2023 midday experiment that drew fewer than 10 because commuters couldn't fit lunch around it.

The series wasn't always the crown jewel. In pre-2023 MAIC we had speakers, but they felt random — a one-off faculty talk here, a random alumnus there. When Ben Paulson restructured the club in 2023, he rebuilt the series around one principle: **be the source of AI news on campus.** Workshops cover current AI advancements, not foundational theory everyone already knows. Speakers cover what their company is actually building with AI, not career-panel platitudes. That shift is why upperclassmen now come to speaker events instead of skipping them.

## Find a speaker

**The best speakers are one warm intro away. Ask the faculty first.**

### How we did it at MAIC

The fastest "yes" we ever got was from our own faculty. Dr. Jeremy Kedziora, Dr. Josiah Yoder, and Dr. Bukowy have all spoken at MAIC multiple times — we ask them when we need a speaker slot filled in under two weeks. The second-fastest yes comes from Dr. Dwight Diercks's NVIDIA network: Ben met Leland Rayner at NVIDIA in May 2024 and had him on our speaker schedule by September. The third easiest is Direct Supply — our primary sponsor sends engineers regularly, coordinated through `Bob Hillis`.

For industry speakers outside our warm network, LinkedIn is the tool. Search for "Applied AI" or "ML Engineer" at Milwaukee companies, check for MSOE alumni, and send a short DM or email. Conference speakers passing through Milwaukee are another pool — we've caught a few when they were in town for Discovery World events or Brady Corp meetings.

### The repeatable version

**Priority order for finding speakers:**

1. **Your own faculty.** Your advisor and any engaged professors. Fastest yes, zero logistics risk.
2. **Sponsor networks.** If you have industry sponsors, ask them for a speaker — they'll usually send someone.
3. **Alumni at tech companies.** Your career services or alumni office can help you find them.
4. **Local industry cold outreach.** LinkedIn search for ML/AI titles at companies within 30 miles.
5. **Conference speakers passing through.** If a big AI event is in your city, some speakers have free slots on either side.

**Outreach template** (adapt our voice):

> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm [Your Name], president of [Club Name] at [University]. We run a bi-weekly speaker series for our ~50-member AI club, focused on what practitioners are actually building with AI — not career-panel stuff.
>
> Would you have 45 minutes on a Thursday evening this fall or spring to present on [specific topic you want them to cover]? We cover travel if you're in the area, and we promote the talk to [university + partner schools] so you'll have a real audience.
>
> Happy to work around your schedule. Let me know what works.
>
> Best,
> [Your Name]

*Adapted from outreach templates Ben Paulson has used with NVIDIA, Brady Corp, and Direct Supply contacts between 2023 and 2024.*

**What to offer in return:**
- Travel reimbursement if they're coming from out of town
- Recording (if they want it for their own use)
- A dedicated audience of 40–50 students (this is the main draw)
- Social media features on your channels

**Always confirm 4 weeks out.** Last-minute speakers mean last-minute promotion, which means a half-empty room. A half-empty room with a brilliant speaker is worse than a full room with a decent one.

### What we'd do differently

We used to book speakers two weeks out and our attendance suffered because promotion was rushed. Build your pipeline so you have the next 3 speakers locked 4+ weeks out at any given time. A single "yes" is worth more if it's 6 weeks out than 2.

## Run the event

**The ITC Great Hall, a mic, free food, and 45+15. That's the whole format.**

### How we did it at MAIC

Every speaker event runs the same way: 6:30pm doors open, food out, mingling. 6:45 Ben (or the current president) does a 2-minute club intro and introduces the speaker. 6:45–7:30 speaker talks (45 minutes). 7:30–7:45 Q&A. 7:45 hard stop — people have lives.

ITC Great Hall logistics are documented in Ben's Board Responsibilities guide:
- Reserve via `Ashley.Vollrath@directsupply.com` with time, attendance estimate, catering Y/N, and any 7400-request needs
- Projector: iPad in back of hall, password `123456`, tap "show visual to stage"
- Microphone: stored in room right of Mario Pipe. Grab one, unmute via the iPad
- Seating: ~120 capacity with standard layout; additional chairs in closet by Diercks Hall entrance

Food is free every event — coordinated through Direct Supply funding (`Bob Hillis` for all-inclusive, `Wade Krogwold` for merch). We default to chicken + sides in fall, pizza in spring.

### The repeatable version

**Pre-event checklist (day of):**
- [ ] Room reserved and confirmed
- [ ] A/V tested (laptop output, mic audio, slide advance)
- [ ] Food ordered and pickup confirmed
- [ ] Speaker confirmed (text them that morning)
- [ ] Intro slide ready (club logo, event title, upcoming events)
- [ ] Announcement posted in Teams / Discord by 9am

**Day-of script (for the intro):**

> Hi everyone, thanks for coming. I'm [name], president of [club]. Before we get started: two things — [1 announcement about upcoming events, 1 call to action like "if you haven't joined our Teams group, scan this QR code"]. Tonight we have [speaker name] from [company], who [one sentence about why they're interesting]. Over to you.

Under 90 seconds. Don't make them wait.

**Q&A management:**
- Seed the first question. Ask an eboard member to have one ready if the room goes silent.
- Time-box the Q&A. 15 minutes max. If questions are still flowing, tell people they can stay after for more.
- Hold the mic yourself or have an eboard runner move it. People won't raise their hand if they have to shout.

**Hard stop at 7:30 (or whenever 60 minutes after you started).** Students have homework, other meetings, dinner plans. Respect the time and they'll come back.

### What we'd do differently

Year one we let Q&A run long because "people were engaged." It made attendance drop the following week because the event started feeling like a time commitment. Hard stop every time. If a speaker is genuinely compelling, move the extra conversation to a smaller room after.

## Build the series

**One-off talks fade. A recurring series becomes part of your club's identity.**

### How we did it at MAIC

Our speaker series is bi-weekly Thursdays throughout the semester. Same day, same time, same room. That consistency is why members know when and where without checking. We tried semester-long theming in 2023 ("safety month," "agents month") and dropped it after one semester — members wanted variety, not a curriculum.

The real work is maintaining the speaker pipeline. Ben kept a running list in `MAIC_Contacts.xlsx` with every past speaker, their company, the topic they covered, and whether they'd said yes to a return visit. When he handed off to Brett, that spreadsheet was half the handoff value. Every speaker gets a "want to do this again next year?" email 30 days after their talk, while the impression is still warm.

Speakers also convert to sponsors. Brady Corp started as a speaker connection through `Matt Vandenbush` (MSOE '98 CE, Brady VP), spoke at MAIC in mid-2024, and became our inaugural Innovation Lab sponsor that fall. Same pattern with Direct Supply — they speak and they fund, and the speaker slots keep both engaged.

### The repeatable version

**Cadence that works:**
- Bi-weekly during academic semesters (not too often, not too rare)
- Same day and time every event
- Alternate weeks with workshops or hands-on project check-ins
- Skip midterm and finals weeks — attendance will tank and it'll feel like a failure

**The pipeline:**
- Maintain a single spreadsheet with every past + prospect speaker
- Columns: Name, Company, Topic, Date Spoken, Status, Return?, Notes
- Always have the next 3 speakers confirmed
- Re-invite past speakers every 18–24 months on a new topic

**Post-event sponsor conversion:**
- Same-week thank-you email to the speaker
- 30-day follow-up: "Would [Company] be open to a sponsorship conversation?"
- If yes, schedule a 30-minute call with their engineering lead or campus recruiting manager
- Sponsorship conversations that start from a speaker relationship close at 3–4× the rate of cold outreach

**Internal archive:**
- Record talks (with speaker permission)
- Post recaps on your club website with 1 paragraph + key takeaways
- Feature the recording in your content library so it doubles as learning content

### What we'd do differently

We should have started recording and archiving earlier. We have recordings from 2024 onward, but the 2022–23 talks are gone. If your speakers agree (most will), hit record from day one. Two years later you'll wish you had.
