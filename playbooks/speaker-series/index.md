# Speaker Series: Bringing Industry to the ITC

**A good speaker series is the lowest-effort, highest-leverage event format we run. Free food, a smart person, a room of students — that's the whole pattern.**

## How we did it at MAIC

We run speaker events in the ITC Great Hall on bi-weekly Thursdays from 6:30–7:30pm. Free food every time. Forty-five minutes of the talk, fifteen for questions, and a hard stop so people can get to the rest of their night. In 2024 alone we hosted 10+ industry speakers — including teams from NVIDIA, Direct Supply, Kohl's, and Generac. Forty to fifty members show up consistently.

The speaker series wasn't always the crown jewel. In the early MAIC days (pre-2023) we had speakers, but they felt random — a one-off talk from a faculty member here, a random alumnus there. When Ben Paulson restructured the club, he rebuilt the speaker series around one principle: **be the source of AI news on campus**. Workshops cover current AI advancements, not foundational theory everyone already knows. Speakers cover what their company is actually building with AI, not career-panel platitudes. That shift is why upperclassmen now come to speaker events instead of skipping them.

Our best-attended event was a Q&A panel with NVIDIA's team in 2024 — 80+ students in a room that comfortably seats 120. Our worst was a midday experiment in 2023 that drew fewer than 10 attendees because commuters couldn't fit lunch around it. Time and format matter as much as the speaker.

## Our actual cadence

Every semester, our speaker series is one piece of a larger arc. Here's how it fits into a MAIC fall:

| Week | Event |
|---|---|
| 1 | Intro to MAIC |
| 2 | Intro to ROSIE (compute access) |
| 2 weekend | NVIDIA DLI Workshop |
| 3 | Hands-On Projects Reveal (Research Groups + Innovation Labs) |
| 5, 7, 9, 11 | Speaker events (bi-weekly Thursdays) |
| ~13 | Hands-On Project Celebration / Hacksgiving |

Speaker events are the connective tissue between intro and the big projects. They keep the community showing up.

## What we'd do differently

We used to book speakers too close to the event — sometimes less than two weeks out — and our attendance suffered because we couldn't promote well. Build your pipeline so you have the next 3 speakers locked 4+ weeks out at any given time. A half-empty room with a brilliant speaker is worse than a full room with a decent one.
