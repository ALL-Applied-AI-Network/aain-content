# Finding and Inviting Speakers

Finding great speakers is the most important part of running a speaker series. The good news: most people are flattered to be asked and will say yes more often than you expect. The key is knowing where to look and how to ask.

## Where to Find Speakers

### Tier 1: Warm Connections (Start Here)
1. **Your own professors and faculty.** They have industry connections, research to share, and are easy to schedule. Ask your AI/ML, CS, or data science faculty first.
2. **Alumni from your program.** Recent grads (1-5 years out) give the most relatable talks for students. Ask your department for alumni contact lists.
3. **People who already recruit on your campus.** Companies that attend career fairs have employees allocated for student engagement.
4. **Parents and family of club members.** Seriously. You would be surprised how many members have a parent or relative in tech.
5. **Other club advisors and their networks.** Your business school's entrepreneur-in-residence, the engineering dean's industry contacts.

### Tier 2: Local Community
6. **Local tech meetup speakers.** People who present at meetups are already comfortable speaking and usually happy to do it again.
7. **Startup founders in your city.** Founders love telling their story. Find them through local incubators, LinkedIn, or AngelList.
8. **Data scientists and ML engineers at local companies.** Reach out on LinkedIn with a personal message.

### Tier 3: Cold Outreach
9. **Conference speakers.** People who have spoken at PyCon, NeurIPS, or local AI conferences are proven speakers. Their talks are often on YouTube -- watch first, then invite.
10. **LinkedIn thought leaders.** People who post regularly about AI/ML often enjoy speaking opportunities.
11. **Authors and researchers.** If someone published a paper or blog post your members loved, invite them to talk about it.
12. **ALL Applied AI Network connections.** Other hubs may have speakers willing to present virtually to your chapter.

## How to Write an Invitation That Gets a Yes

### The Cold Outreach Email

> **Subject:** Speaking invitation: [Club Name] at [University] -- [Topic area]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm [Your Name], [role] at [Club Name], the AI/applied ML student organization at [University]. We run a bi-weekly speaker series for [X] members interested in applied AI.
>
> I came across your [work on X / talk at Y / post about Z] and think our members would benefit hugely from hearing your perspective on [specific topic].
>
> **The ask:** A [30-40 minute] talk followed by Q&A, either in-person at our campus or over Zoom -- whatever works best for you. We are flexible on dates and can work around your schedule.
>
> **What we offer:**
> - An engaged audience of [X] students studying [relevant fields]
> - A recorded session we can share to amplify your reach (optional, your choice)
> - Dinner or coffee with our leadership team before/after the talk (in-person)
> - Exposure to talented students (several of our alumni are now at [notable companies])
>
> Would you be open to this? I am happy to hop on a quick call to discuss details.
>
> Best,
> [Your Name]
> [Club Name] | [University]
> [Website or social link]

### What Makes This Email Work
- **Specific.** References their actual work, not a generic template.
- **Short.** Respectable people are busy. Get to the point.
- **Low commitment.** "30-40 minutes" and "flexible on dates" reduces friction.
- **Clear value exchange.** They see what they get (audience, exposure, connection to students).

### LinkedIn Direct Message (Shorter Version)

> Hi [Name], I lead [Club Name] at [University] -- we're a student AI org with [X] active members. Your [work/post] on [topic] really resonated with our group. Would you be interested in giving a [30-min talk or fireside chat] for our members? Happy to work around your schedule and do this over Zoom if easier. Would love to chat if you're open to it.

## How Far in Advance to Reach Out

| Speaker Type | Lead Time |
|-------------|-----------|
| Faculty at your university | 2-3 weeks |
| Local professionals | 3-4 weeks |
| Cold outreach (industry) | 4-6 weeks |
| High-profile speakers | 6-8+ weeks |

**Tip:** Build a pipeline. Always be reaching out 4-6 weeks ahead so you are never scrambling for next week's speaker.

## Follow-Up Cadence

1. **Initial email sent.** Wait 5 business days.
2. **First follow-up:** Short and friendly. "Just bumping this in case it got buried -- would love to have you."
3. **Second follow-up (1 week later):** Final attempt. "Totally understand if the timing doesn't work. We'd love to keep you in mind for a future semester."
4. **No response after 3 attempts:** Move on. Do not take it personally.

**Response rate benchmarks:** Expect 20-30% response rate on cold outreach, 50-70% on warm introductions. Of those who respond, about 60-80% will say yes.

## What to Offer Speakers

Most speakers at student events will present for free. But showing appreciation matters:

| Offering | Cost | Impact |
|----------|------|--------|
| Thank-you email with event photos | Free | Expected minimum |
| Social media feature and tag | Free | High value for speakers building their personal brand |
| Dinner or coffee with leadership team | $30-50 | Great for relationship building |
| Small gift (book, university merch) | $15-30 | Thoughtful and memorable |
| Donation to their preferred charity | $25-50 | Classy alternative to a gift |
| Honorarium (stipend) | $100-500 | Only for high-profile speakers; check university policies |

**Never offer to pay before exploring free options.** Most industry professionals do not expect payment for a student talk.

## Building a Speaker Pipeline

Do not treat each talk as a one-off search. Build a system:

1. **Maintain a speaker tracker spreadsheet:** Name, company, topic, contact info, status (contacted, confirmed, completed, declined), notes
2. **After every talk, ask the speaker:** "Is there anyone you'd recommend we invite?" Personal referrals have 2-3x the acceptance rate of cold outreach.
3. **Keep a wish list:** When members mention someone they admire, add them to the list.
4. **Reconnect with past speakers:** They may have new material, or they can introduce you to colleagues.
5. **Coordinate with the ALL Applied AI Network:** Share speaker contacts across hubs to reduce duplication and increase reach.

## Speaker Pre-Event Checklist

Once a speaker says yes:

- [ ] Confirm date, time, and format (in-person or virtual)
- [ ] Schedule a 15-minute prep call 1-2 weeks before the talk
- [ ] Ask if they need any specific A/V setup or software
- [ ] Ask if they want the talk recorded and shared (get explicit consent)
- [ ] Collect their bio, headshot, and talk title for promotion
- [ ] Send them audience context: who attends, experience level, what topics resonate
- [ ] Confirm logistics: room location, parking, Zoom link, etc.
- [ ] Send a reminder 48 hours before with all details in one email
