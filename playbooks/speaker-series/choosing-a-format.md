# Choosing a Format

Not all talks are the same. The format you choose shapes the audience experience, the amount of prep required from your speaker, and how much your members get out of the event. Pick the format that matches your goals, your speaker's strengths, and your audience size.

## Format Options

### Single-Speaker Technical Talk
- **What it is:** One speaker presents on a technical topic for 30-45 minutes, followed by 15-20 minutes of Q&A.
- **Best for:** Deep dives into specific technologies, research presentations, project walkthroughs
- **Audience size:** 15-80 people
- **Effort level:** Low (speaker does most of the prep)
- **Tip:** This is your default format. It works for almost any speaker and audience. Start here.

### Panel Discussion
- **What it is:** 3-4 speakers sit together, moderated by a host, discussing a shared topic. Usually 45-60 minutes.
- **Best for:** Broad topics like "careers in AI," "ethics of generative AI," or "industry vs. academia"
- **Audience size:** 30-100 people
- **Effort level:** Medium (you need to recruit multiple speakers and prepare moderator questions)
- **Tip:** The moderator makes or breaks a panel. Choose someone who can keep the conversation moving, redirect long answers, and draw out quieter panelists.

### Fireside Chat
- **What it is:** An informal, interview-style conversation between a host and one speaker. Usually 30-40 minutes plus audience Q&A.
- **Best for:** High-profile speakers, career stories, founders sharing their journey
- **Audience size:** 20-60 people (more intimate feel)
- **Effort level:** Medium (host needs to prepare strong questions)
- **Tip:** Fireside chats feel casual but require more host preparation than a standard talk. Write 15-20 questions and expect to use 8-10. The best fireside chats feel like eavesdropping on a great conversation.

### Lightning Talks
- **What it is:** 4-6 speakers each give a 5-7 minute talk on different topics. No Q&A between talks; optional group Q&A at the end.
- **Best for:** Showcasing club members' projects, exposing the audience to many topics quickly, low barrier for first-time speakers
- **Audience size:** 15-50 people
- **Tip:** Lightning talks are excellent for building a speaking culture within your club. Run an internal lightning talk night before inviting external speakers.

### AMA (Ask Me Anything)
- **What it is:** The speaker gives a brief (5-10 minute) introduction, then the rest is audience-driven Q&A for 30-45 minutes.
- **Best for:** Very experienced speakers with strong opinions, career advice sessions, informal networking events
- **Audience size:** 10-40 people (smaller is better for AMAs)
- **Effort level:** Low (but you need a plan for seeding questions)
- **Tip:** Always have 10 prepared questions ready in case the audience is slow to start. Awkward silence kills an AMA.

## Format Comparison

| Format | Prep Time | Speaker Prep | Best Audience Size | Energy Level |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|--------------|
| Technical talk | Low | High | 15-80 | Medium |
| Panel | Medium | Low per panelist | 30-100 | High |
| Fireside chat | Medium | Low | 20-60 | Medium-High |
| Lightning talks | Medium | Low per speaker | 15-50 | High |
| AMA | Low | Minimal | 10-40 | Variable |

## Virtual vs. In-Person

### In-Person
- Better for networking and community building
- Easier to manage Q&A (raise hands, eye contact)
- Higher energy in the room
- **Choose this when:** You have a good venue and the speaker is local or willing to travel

### Virtual (Zoom, Google Meet, Discord Stage)
- Expands your reach to anyone (other campuses, remote members, speakers anywhere in the world)
- Easier to record
- Lower attendance commitment means higher no-show rates
- **Choose this when:** The speaker is remote, you want to reach a broader audience, or your campus lacks good event space

### Hybrid
- Technically challenging to do well -- the remote audience often feels like an afterthought
- Requires dedicated A/V setup and someone monitoring the virtual chat
- **Choose this only if:** You have the tech setup and a dedicated person managing the virtual side

**Recommendation:** Default to in-person for local speakers, virtual for remote speakers. Avoid hybrid unless you have tested the setup beforehand.

## Choosing the Right Length

| Event Type | Total Duration | Talk/Content | Q&A |
|------------|---------------|-------------|-----|
| Quick talk | 30-40 min | 20-25 min | 10-15 min |
| Standard talk | 50-60 min | 30-40 min | 15-20 min |
| Panel/fireside | 60-75 min | 40-50 min | 15-25 min |
| Lightning talks | 45-60 min | 30-40 min (total) | 10-15 min (group) |

**Tip:** 50-60 minutes total is the sweet spot for most events. Shorter than 30 minutes feels rushed. Longer than 75 minutes and attention drops significantly.

## Decision Checklist

- [ ] Format selected based on speaker strengths and audience goals
- [ ] Duration decided
- [ ] In-person, virtual, or hybrid determined
- [ ] Moderator/host identified (if panel or fireside)
- [ ] Q&A strategy planned (see [Running the Event](./running-the-event.md))
- [ ] Communicated format expectations to the speaker

## Common Mistakes

1. **Defaulting to panels when a single speaker would be better.** Panels are harder to organize and often dilute the message. Use them intentionally.
2. **Running too long.** Respect your audience's time. End 5 minutes early rather than 5 minutes late.
3. **Not matching the format to the speaker.** A quiet, thoughtful researcher will shine in a fireside chat but struggle in a high-energy panel. Ask the speaker what they are comfortable with.
4. **Skipping Q&A.** Q&A is often the most valuable part for the audience. Always leave time for it.
