# Promotion and Attendance

An empty room is a speaker's worst nightmare and your biggest avoidable failure. Promotion is not optional. Even a well-known speaker at a convenient time will draw a small crowd if nobody hears about it. Here is how to fill the room consistently.

## The Single Most Effective Tactic

**Personal invitations.** Nothing beats a club member personally telling a classmate, "We have a talk on Thursday about how Company X uses ML in production. You should come." Text messages, DMs, and face-to-face invitations convert at 5-10x the rate of social media posts.

Make personal outreach a team effort. At your weekly meeting, ask each officer to personally invite 3-5 people. If 5 officers each bring 3 friends, that is 15 attendees from personal invitations alone.

## Social Media Strategy

### Instagram
- **2 weeks before:** Announcement post with speaker photo, name, topic, date/time. Use a clean graphic (Canva templates work great).
- **1 week before:** Story with speaker bio and "what you'll learn" bullets.
- **Day before:** Story reminder with "tomorrow" urgency.
- **Day of:** Story 1-2 hours before with room location.
- **During/after:** Photos and key quotes for a recap post.

### Discord/Slack
- **2 weeks before:** Announcement in #announcements with full details and a reaction emoji for RSVPs (helps you gauge interest).
- **3 days before:** Reminder ping.
- **Day of, morning:** "Tonight's talk" reminder with logistics.
- **1 hour before:** "Starting soon" message.

### LinkedIn
- Best for talks by industry speakers. Post the announcement and tag the speaker.
- The speaker may reshare it, expanding your reach to their professional network.
- Great for building club credibility with industry.

### Email List
- Send to your club mailing list 1 week before and again 1 day before.
- Keep the email short: who is speaking, what they will talk about, when, where, and a link to RSVP.
- Subject line matters: "[Club Name] Talk: [Speaker Name] on [Catchy Topic]" beats "[Club Name] Weekly Event."

## Class Announcements

One of the most underused promotion channels. Here is how to use it:

1. Identify 3-5 relevant classes (intro to AI, data science, software engineering, etc.)
2. Email the professor 1 week before: "Would you be willing to give us 30 seconds at the start of class to announce a talk related to your course?"
3. Prepare a 30-second script: "Hey everyone, I'm [Name] from [Club]. This Thursday at [time] we have [Speaker] from [Company] talking about [topic]. It's free and open to everyone. Here's the link." Drop a flyer or write the link on the board.
4. Mention how the talk connects to course material. Professors are more likely to allow it if it is relevant.

**Tip:** Ask your faculty advisor to mention it in their classes. A professor endorsement carries weight.

## Flyers and Physical Promotion

Physical flyers still work, especially in engineering and CS buildings:

- Print 15-20 color flyers (use your school's free printing if available)
- Post on bulletin boards in CS, engineering, math, and business buildings
- Include: speaker name, topic, date, time, location, QR code linking to more info
- Post flyers 1 week before the event. Earlier and they get buried; later and people cannot plan.

## Cross-Promotion with Other Clubs

Reach out to related student organizations:

- Data science club
- Robotics club
- Entrepreneurship or startup club
- Business analytics club
- Pre-professional engineering societies
- Women in tech / diversity in STEM groups

**What to offer:** "We'll promote your next event to our members if you promote ours." Reciprocal promotion costs nothing and expands both audiences.

## RSVP and Attendance Tracking

### Should You Require RSVPs?
- RSVPs help you estimate attendance (plan for 50-60% of RSVPs to show)
- Use Luma, Google Forms, or a simple Discord reaction for low-friction RSVPs
- Do not require registration for regular talks -- it adds friction and reduces attendance
- Consider RSVPs for special events (dinner with speaker, limited-capacity venues)

### Tracking Attendance
- Take a headcount at each event (useful for reports to sponsors and university)
- A sign-in sheet or QR code check-in works for formal tracking
- Track attendance trends over the semester to see what topics and formats draw the most people

## Attendance Goals by Club Size

| Club Mailing List | Realistic Attendance | Good Attendance |
|-------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| 50 members | 10-15 | 20+ |
| 100 members | 15-25 | 35+ |
| 200 members | 25-40 | 50+ |
| 300+ members | 35-60 | 75+ |

If you are consistently below "realistic" numbers, your promotion is the bottleneck, not interest. Double down on personal invitations and class announcements.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Promoting only on one channel.** Not everyone checks Discord. Not everyone follows Instagram. Use at least 3 channels.
2. **Promoting too late.** People need 3-5 days to plan. A day-of announcement is a last resort, not a strategy.
3. **Generic promotion.** "Come to our talk" is less compelling than "Learn how [Company] uses transformers to detect fraud in real-time."
4. **Not asking members to bring friends.** Explicitly ask. "Bring one friend who has never been to a club event."
5. **Ignoring walk-in traffic.** If your event is in a high-traffic building, put a sign outside the door: "AI Talk happening now -- all welcome."
