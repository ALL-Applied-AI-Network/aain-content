# Running the Event

You have booked the room, promoted the talk, and the speaker is on their way. Now it is about execution. A smooth event feels effortless to attendees, but that ease comes from preparation and attention to the small things.

## 30 Minutes Before: Setup

- [ ] Arrive at the room and unlock it
- [ ] Turn on projector/screen, test display
- [ ] Set up speaker's laptop or confirm your adapter works with theirs
- [ ] Test audio (microphone, speakers) if the room needs amplification
- [ ] Set up recording equipment if applicable
- [ ] Arrange chairs if needed (fill front rows first -- do not let everyone sit in the back)
- [ ] Put out food and drinks (at the back of the room)
- [ ] Write on the whiteboard or post a sign: event name, Wi-Fi password, club social handles
- [ ] Start the Zoom/virtual meeting link if hybrid

## Greeting the Speaker

- Meet the speaker at the building entrance or a designated spot (do not make them wander)
- Walk them to the room, make small talk, help them settle in
- Offer water (have a bottle ready at the podium or speaker table)
- Help them connect to the projector and test their slides
- Do a quick audio check: "Can you hear me in the back?"
- Confirm timing: "You've got 35 minutes for the talk, then we'll open it up for 15 minutes of Q&A. I'll give you a 5-minute warning."

**Tip:** Speakers are often nervous, even experienced ones. A warm, organized greeting makes a big difference.

## The Introduction

Keep it under 2 minutes. Do not read their entire LinkedIn profile.

### Introduction Template

> Thanks for coming out tonight. I'm [Your Name] from [Club Name].
>
> Tonight we have [Speaker Name], who is a [role] at [Company]. [One sentence about their relevant background or why we invited them]. [One sentence connecting their expertise to something the audience cares about].
>
> [Speaker Name], thanks for being here. Take it away.

### Example

> Thanks for coming out tonight. I'm Alex from the Applied AI Club.
>
> Tonight we have Sarah Chen, a senior ML engineer at Spotify. Sarah leads the team that builds the recommendation models behind Discover Weekly, and she was one of the first ML hires at a startup that was acquired by Spotify in 2022.
>
> Sarah, thanks for being here. Take it away.

## Time Management

- Assign someone (not the host) to track time
- Give the speaker a 5-minute warning (hold up a phone with "5 min" on the screen or send a subtle signal)
- If the speaker is running over, wait for a natural pause and step in: "That's a great point. We want to make sure we have time for questions -- should we open it up?"
- Never cut a speaker off mid-sentence. Find a graceful transition.

## Managing Q&A

Q&A is where talks either come alive or die in awkward silence. Your job as host is to make sure neither extreme happens.

### Seeding Questions

**Always have 3-5 prepared questions ready.** These are your insurance policy against silence.

Examples of good seed questions:
- "What does a typical day look like in your role?"
- "What is a common misconception about [topic] that you wish more people understood?"
- "If you were a student today, what would you focus on learning?"
- "What was the hardest technical challenge you faced on [project they mentioned]?"

### Opening Q&A

> "We have about 15 minutes for questions. Who wants to go first?"
>
> [If silence for more than 5 seconds]
>
> "I'll kick us off -- [ask your first prepared question]"

After the first question is answered, audience questions usually flow more naturally.

### Managing the Flow

- Repeat each question into the microphone (or loudly) so everyone hears it
- If a question is vague or too long, help refine it: "So the question is essentially, [rephrased version] -- is that right?"
- If someone is asking a very niche or personal question, say: "Great question -- maybe you two can connect after the talk on that one. Next question?"
- Watch the clock. When 3 minutes remain: "We have time for one more question."

### Handling Difficult Situations

| Situation | How to Handle |
|-----------|--------------|
| No questions at all | Use your prepared questions. After 2-3, the audience usually warms up. |
| One person dominating Q&A | After their second question: "Great questions. Let's hear from someone who hasn't had a chance yet." |
| Hostile or aggressive question | Stay calm. "That's an interesting perspective. [Speaker], how would you respond?" Let the speaker handle the substance. |
| Question that is really a monologue | After 20 seconds: "What's the question you'd like [Speaker] to address?" |
| Speaker gives a very long answer | During a natural pause: "That's really helpful. Let's take another question." |

## Closing the Event

When Q&A time is up:

> "Let's give [Speaker Name] a round of applause for an excellent talk."
>
> [Applause]
>
> "If you want to continue the conversation, [Speaker] will be around for a few minutes after. Feel free to come up and say hi."
>
> "A few quick announcements: [next event, how to join the club, any relevant links]. Thanks for coming out, and we'll see you at [next event date]."

## Post-Talk Networking

The 10-15 minutes after a talk are valuable networking time:

- Encourage members to approach the speaker (some students need a nudge)
- As host, introduce specific members to the speaker: "Sarah, this is Jordan -- they are working on a recommendation system for their capstone project."
- If the speaker seems overwhelmed by a crowd, help manage the line: "Sarah has time for a few more conversations."
- Offer to walk the speaker out when they are ready to leave

## Hybrid Event Specifics

If you are running a hybrid event with virtual attendees:

- [ ] Assign one person to manage the Zoom chat and relay questions
- [ ] Position the camera so virtual attendees can see the speaker and slides
- [ ] Use an external microphone (not the laptop mic) for clear audio
- [ ] Monitor the chat for technical issues ("I can't hear" or "screen is frozen")
- [ ] Include virtual attendees in Q&A by alternating between in-room and chat questions
- [ ] Thank virtual attendees during closing

## Day-Of Checklist

### Before the Event
- [ ] Room open and set up (30 min before)
- [ ] A/V tested
- [ ] Speaker greeted and settled
- [ ] Recording started (if applicable)
- [ ] Food set out

### During the Event
- [ ] Introduction delivered (under 2 minutes)
- [ ] Time tracked, 5-minute warning given
- [ ] Q&A facilitated with seed questions ready
- [ ] Photos taken (at least 2-3 for social media)

### After the Event
- [ ] Speaker thanked personally
- [ ] Room cleaned up and locked
- [ ] Recording stopped and saved
- [ ] Quick notes written: attendance count, audience engagement, any feedback
