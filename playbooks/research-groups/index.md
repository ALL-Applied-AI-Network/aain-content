# Research Groups: From Curiosity to Publication

Research groups give your most motivated members a path that goes deeper than workshops or hackathons. A well-organized research group takes students from reading papers to producing original work, building skills that matter whether they pursue academia or industry.

This is not about replicating a graduate research lab. It is about creating a structured, supportive environment where undergraduates can engage with the research process: identify questions, review literature, design experiments, analyze results, and communicate findings. Some groups will target conference submissions. Others will produce technical blog posts or open-source tools. Both are valuable.

## Who This Is For

A hub leader who has members asking for more depth. You do not need a PhD advisor to start (though having one helps). You need 3-8 committed students willing to invest 3-5 hours per week over a semester, and at least one person with enough technical background to guide the group's direction.

## What Success Looks Like

- Members develop the ability to read and critically evaluate AI research papers
- The group produces at least one tangible output per semester (paper, blog post, open-source project, or technical report)
- Students build mentorship relationships with faculty or industry researchers
- Members gain experience that differentiates them in graduate school or job applications

## Getting Started Checklist

- [ ] Identify 3-5 potential research topics based on member interest
- [ ] Recruit 3-8 committed members
- [ ] Establish a weekly meeting time (90 minutes minimum)
- [ ] Set up a shared document repository (Notion, Google Drive, or GitHub)
- [ ] Read and discuss 2-3 papers as a group before committing to a direction
- [ ] Reach out to at least one potential faculty mentor
- [ ] Define your semester goal and target output format
