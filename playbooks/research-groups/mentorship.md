# Finding Mentorship

A good mentor accelerates your research group by months. They help you avoid dead ends, ask better questions, and connect your work to the broader research community. This page covers how to find mentors, how to approach them, and how to structure the relationship so it works for everyone.

## Types of Mentors

### Faculty Advisor
A professor at your university who guides your research direction, provides feedback on methodology, and may eventually serve as a co-author or reference.

**Best for**: Groups targeting a publication or conference submission. Groups that want formal academic research experience.

### Industry Researcher
A data scientist, ML engineer, or research scientist at a company who can provide practical perspective on your work.

**Best for**: Groups working on applied problems. Groups that want industry connections.

### Graduate Student Mentor
A PhD or Master's student in a related area who can help with day-to-day technical questions.

**Best for**: Groups that need frequent, low-barrier technical help. A grad student is often more accessible than a professor.

### Network Mentor
A researcher or practitioner connected through the ALL Applied AI Network who can provide guidance remotely.

**Best for**: Groups at schools without strong AI faculty. Groups working on niche topics where the closest expert is not local.

You do not need all four. One dedicated mentor is better than four who are loosely engaged.

## How to Approach Faculty

### Finding the Right Person
1. Look at your university's CS, Data Science, Statistics, or Engineering departments
2. Read faculty research pages. Look for people whose work overlaps with your topic.
3. Check who teaches AI/ML courses. They may not do research in your specific area, but they understand the process.
4. Ask other students. "Who is approachable?" is a valid and important question.

### The Cold Email

Faculty receive dozens of emails per week. Make yours easy to say yes to.

```
Subject: Undergraduate AI Research Group - Seeking Advisor

Professor [Name],

I am [your name], a [year] in [major] at [university]. I lead a
research group of [X] students in the [club name], and we are
working on [one-sentence description of your research direction].

We have been reading papers on [specific topic] and are planning
experiments on [specific plan]. We are looking for a faculty
advisor who could meet with us [frequency, e.g., biweekly for
30 minutes] to provide guidance on our research direction and
methodology.

I noticed your work on [specific paper or project of theirs].
[One sentence about why their expertise is relevant to your work.]

We are not asking for funding or lab space. We have our own
meeting time and compute resources. We are looking for
intellectual guidance and feedback.

Would you have 15 minutes for an introductory meeting? I am
happy to come to your office at whatever time works for you.

Thank you,
[Your name]
[Your email and role in the club]
```

### What Makes This Email Work
- It is specific. You are not asking them to "help with AI." You have a direction.
- It is low-commitment. Biweekly 30-minute meetings is reasonable.
- It shows you have done your homework on their work.
- It does not ask for money or resources.
- It is short.

### If They Say No
- Ask if they can recommend another faculty member who might be a better fit
- Ask if they would be willing to give feedback on a specific piece of work (a one-time review is easier to agree to than an ongoing commitment)
- Try another faculty member. Rejection is normal. It usually means they are busy, not that your project is bad.

### If They Say Yes
- Come to the first meeting prepared: bring your research question, reading list, and experiment plan
- Take notes and send a follow-up email with action items
- Respect their time. End meetings on time or early. Never show up without having made progress since the last meeting.

## How to Approach Industry Researchers

### Where to Find Them
- LinkedIn: Search for ML engineers and data scientists at companies in your area
- Your club's alumni network: Graduates working in AI roles
- Meetups and conferences: Local data science or AI meetups
- The ALL Applied AI Network: Use the network's mentorship matching if available
- Speaker follow-ups: If someone gives a talk at your club, ask if they would be open to ongoing mentorship

### The Ask
Industry researchers are often eager to mentor but have never been asked. Your pitch:

- "We are a group of [X] undergraduates working on [topic]. Would you be open to a monthly 30-minute check-in where we share our progress and get your feedback?"
- Offer to meet at their convenience: video call, coffee near their office, or async via email
- Emphasize that you are organized and will make the meetings productive (they are donating their time)

## Structuring the Mentor Relationship

### Meeting Cadence
- **Faculty advisor**: Biweekly, 30 minutes (can shift to weekly during crunch periods)
- **Industry mentor**: Monthly, 30 minutes (with async questions between meetings)
- **Grad student mentor**: Weekly, 15-30 minutes (or async via Slack/Discord)

### Before Each Meeting
Send your mentor a brief update 24 hours in advance:

```markdown
## Research Group Update - [Date]

### Progress since last meeting
- [Completed task 1]
- [Completed task 2]

### Current blockers
- [What we are stuck on]

### Questions for this meeting
1. [Specific question 1]
2. [Specific question 2]

### Plan for next two weeks
- [Planned task 1]
- [Planned task 2]
```

This shows respect for their time and ensures the meeting is productive.

### What to Ask Your Mentor
Good questions:
- "We are deciding between approach A and approach B. Here is our reasoning. What do you think?"
- "Our results on X seem inconsistent. Here is what we have tried. What would you check next?"
- "We want to submit to [conference/workshop]. Is our work a good fit? What would strengthen it?"

Less productive questions:
- "What should we work on?" (You should have a proposal; ask them to react to it)
- "Can you debug our code?" (That is not a mentor's job. Ask specific technical questions instead.)
- "Is this good?" (Too vague. Ask about specific aspects.)

## Maintaining the Relationship

### Show Gratitude
- Thank them after every meeting
- Acknowledge their contributions in any public output (papers, blog posts, presentations)
- Send a semester-end thank-you with a summary of what the group accomplished
- If a group member gets an opportunity (internship, grad school admission) that their mentorship helped with, tell them

### Give Back
- Offer to present your work at a department seminar or lab meeting (faculty love this)
- Share useful papers or tools you discover during your research
- If the mentor has students, offer to help with peer feedback
- Write a testimonial about their mentorship for their portfolio or tenure file

### When It Is Not Working
Sometimes the fit is not right. Signs to watch for:
- The mentor consistently cancels or reschedules
- Feedback is consistently unhelpful or too generic
- The mentor is pushing the group toward their own research interests rather than supporting yours
- Communication feels strained

If this happens, have an honest conversation. If things do not improve, it is okay to thank them for their time and find a different mentor. Do this gracefully. The research community is small.

## No Mentor? Keep Going.

If you cannot find a mentor, you can still run a productive research group:

- Lean on the paper reading pipeline to build expertise
- Use online communities (Reddit r/MachineLearning, Twitter/X ML community, Discord servers) to ask questions
- Attend virtual talks and office hours at conferences (many are free and open to undergraduates)
- Connect with other hubs in the ALL Applied AI Network that may have mentors willing to advise remotely
- A mentor makes things easier, but committed students with good process can produce real work on their own
