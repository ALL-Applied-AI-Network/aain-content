# Presenting and Sharing Work

Research that nobody sees might as well not exist. Presenting your work builds your group's credibility, develops essential communication skills, and creates opportunities. This page covers how to present to different audiences, from your own club to a conference stage.

## Internal Club Presentations

### Why Do These First
- Low-stakes practice for higher-stakes presentations
- Gets your club members excited about research (recruiting for next semester)
- Forces you to explain your work clearly to a non-expert audience
- Identifies gaps in your own understanding (if you cannot explain it, you do not fully understand it)

### Format
- **Length**: 15-20 minutes plus 10 minutes Q&A
- **Audience**: Club members who may have basic AI knowledge but no context on your specific work
- **Slides**: 10-15 slides
- **Tone**: Accessible. No jargon without explanation.

### Suggested Structure
1. **The big picture** (2 min): What area of AI are you working in and why does it matter?
2. **Your specific question** (2 min): What are you trying to figure out?
3. **What others have done** (2 min): Brief context on existing approaches
4. **Your approach** (4 min): What did you build or test? Use diagrams.
5. **Results** (3 min): What happened? Show the key figure or table.
6. **What you learned** (2 min): Insights, surprises, and open questions
7. **What is next** (1 min): Where is the project going?

### Tips
- Start with a concrete example or story, not a definition
- Use visuals over text. One clear diagram beats three bullet points.
- Practice the timing. Undergraduate presenters almost always run long.
- End with a question for the audience to encourage discussion

## University Research Symposia

Most universities host an annual undergraduate research symposium or poster session. This is a natural target for your group.

### Poster Presentations

**Poster layout** (standard 48" x 36"):

```
+--------------------------------------------------+
| TITLE (large, readable from 6 feet away)          |
| Authors and Affiliations                          |
+--------------------------------------------------+
|           |              |              |         |
| Intro &   |  Method      |  Results     | Conclu- |
| Motivation|  (with       |  (key figure | sions & |
|           |  diagram)    |  or table)   | Future  |
|           |              |              | Work    |
+--------------------------------------------------+
| References | Acknowledgments | QR code to repo    |
+--------------------------------------------------+
```

**Poster tips**:
- Less text is more. Use bullet points, not paragraphs.
- Your most important figure should be the visual centerpiece
- Font size: title 72pt+, section headers 36pt+, body text 24pt+
- Include a QR code linking to your code, paper, or blog post
- Print a test version at letter size first to check for errors

**Presenting at the poster**:
- Prepare a 2-minute "elevator pitch" version of your work
- Prepare a 5-minute detailed walkthrough for interested visitors
- Stand next to your poster, not behind it
- Make eye contact and engage visitors: "Would you like to hear about our project?"
- Have all group members rotate poster duty so everyone practices

### Oral Presentations at Symposia

- Typically 10-15 minutes
- More formal than club presentations but less formal than conferences
- Focus on clarity over comprehensiveness
- Practice at least 3 times before the event

## Conference Presentations

If your paper is accepted at a workshop or conference, you will likely present as a poster or give a short talk.

### Conference Poster Sessions
Same format as university posters but with a more technical audience. Adjust your pitch:
- Skip basic background (assume the audience knows the field)
- Emphasize your specific contribution and results
- Be ready for detailed technical questions
- Have the paper available (printed copies or a QR code)

### Conference Talks (5-10 minutes typical for workshops)

These are short. Every second counts.

| Slide | Content | Time |
|-------|---------|------|
| 1 | Title and authors | 10 sec |
| 2 | Problem: what and why | 45 sec |
| 3 | Key insight of your approach | 1 min |
| 4-5 | Method: the essentials only | 2 min |
| 6-7 | Results: your best figure and table | 2 min |
| 8 | Analysis: what works, what does not | 1 min |
| 9 | Conclusion and future work | 30 sec |

**Conference talk tips**:
- Do not try to cover everything in the paper. Focus on the one thing you want the audience to remember.
- Rehearse with a timer. If you go over, cut content (do not talk faster).
- End with a clear takeaway, not "any questions?"
- Bring backup slides for anticipated Q&A questions

## Technical Blog Posts

Blog posts reach a wider audience than papers and build your group's public profile.

### Structure for a Research Blog Post

```markdown
# [Catchy but accurate title]

**TL;DR**: [2-3 sentence summary of what you did and what you found]

## The Problem
[Why does this matter? Use an example a non-expert can relate to.]

## What We Tried
[Your approach, explained with diagrams and intuition rather than
equations. Link to the paper for formal details.]

## What We Found
[Key results with clear visualizations. Explain what the numbers mean.]

## What Surprised Us
[The most interesting finding. This is what makes people share the post.]

## Try It Yourself
[Link to code, notebook, or demo. Lower the barrier to engagement.]

## What Is Next
[Where is this going? What questions remain?]
```

### Where to Publish
- **Your club's website**: Best for building the club's brand
- **Personal blogs**: Best for building individual portfolios
- **Medium / Towards Data Science**: Larger built-in audience, but you do not control the platform
- **dev.to or Hashnode**: Developer-oriented audiences

### Writing Tips
- Write for someone who has taken one ML class. Not a complete beginner, not an expert.
- Use first person plural ("we found" not "it was found")
- Include code snippets for key implementation details
- Every figure needs a caption that explains what the reader should take away
- Keep it under 2,000 words. If it is longer, consider splitting into a series.

## Sharing on Social Media

After you publish or present, amplify it:

### LinkedIn Post Template
```
Our research group at [university] just [presented/published/released]
our work on [topic].

We [brief description of what you did and what you found].

[Key insight or surprising finding in one sentence.]

[Link to paper/blog/code]

Thanks to [mentor, sponsor, club] for supporting this work.

#MachineLearning #AI #UndergraduateResearch
```

### Twitter/X Thread Template
```
1/ Our @[club handle] research group spent the semester working on
[topic]. Here is what we found. [thread emoji]

2/ The question: [research question in plain language]

3/ Our approach: [1-2 sentences + a figure]

4/ The key result: [the one number or finding that matters most]

5/ The surprise: [what you did not expect]

6/ Code and paper: [links]
```

## Building a Portfolio

Every presentation and publication should become a portfolio piece:

- [ ] Add the project to your personal website or GitHub profile
- [ ] Update your resume with the project title, methods used, and any publication/presentation
- [ ] Add the publication or presentation to your LinkedIn profile
- [ ] Save the slides, poster, and any recordings for future reference
- [ ] If the code is public, make sure the README is clear and the repo is well-organized

For graduate school applications, a published workshop paper or a strong blog post with code is worth more than a 4.0 GPA in courses alone. For industry roles, a public project with clean code and clear results demonstrates exactly the skills that hiring managers look for.
