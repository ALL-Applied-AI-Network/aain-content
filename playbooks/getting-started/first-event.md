# Designing Your First Event

Your first event is the most important one you will ever run. It sets the tone for everything that follows. People will decide within 30 minutes whether your club is worth their time. This section covers event formats that work for brand-new clubs, how to scope content so beginners feel welcome, and how to make sure people leave wanting to come back.

## The Goal of Your First Event

Your first event has exactly two objectives:

1. **Give attendees a tangible win.** They should leave having built or created something real, no matter their skill level.
2. **Make them want to come back.** The second meeting is harder to fill than the first. Design for retention, not just attendance.

Notice what is not on this list: impressing people with advanced content, covering a comprehensive curriculum, or getting through a ton of material. Those are traps.

## Choosing the Right Format

Not all event formats work equally well for a first meeting. Here is a ranked breakdown based on what actually drives retention in new clubs.

| Format | First-event fit | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Hands-on workshop** | Best | Everyone builds something. Creates shared experience. |
| **Build night / hack session** | Good | Works if attendees have some baseline skill. Less structured. |
| **Panel or Q&A** | Decent | Low effort to organize but passive for attendees. |
| **Lecture or presentation** | Avoid | Feels like class. People came for something different. |
| **Social mixer** | Avoid for first event | No substance to evaluate. Save for later. |

## Designing a Workshop That Works

A good first workshop has four properties:

1. **Zero prerequisites.** Anyone with a laptop can participate.
2. **Fast time-to-first-result.** Attendees should see something working within the first 15 minutes.
3. **Clear scope.** One topic, one deliverable, done in 60-90 minutes.
4. **Graduated difficulty.** Everyone hits the baseline. Faster students have stretch goals to pursue.

### Recommended first workshop topics

These topics have been validated across multiple ALL Applied AI Network hubs:

- **Build a chatbot with an AI API.** Students make API calls to a language model and build a conversational interface. Works for all skill levels -- beginners use a provided template, advanced students customize the system prompt and add features.
- **Prompt engineering challenge.** No coding required. Students compete to get the best outputs from an AI model for a series of increasingly complex tasks. Great for mixed-skill audiences.
- **AI-powered app in 60 minutes.** Using an AI IDE like Cursor, students build a small web application from scratch with AI assistance. Demonstrates the "applied AI" philosophy immediately.

### Workshop structure template

Here is a tested structure for a 90-minute first workshop:

```
FIRST WORKSHOP AGENDA (90 minutes)

0:00 - 0:10  Welcome and introductions
             - Who we are, what this club is about (keep it to 3 minutes)
             - Quick icebreaker: "What is the coolest thing you have
               seen AI do?" (go around the room, 30 seconds each)

0:10 - 0:20  Setup and context
             - Make sure everyone has the tools installed / accounts created
             - 2-minute explanation of what we are building and why it matters

0:20 - 0:50  Guided build (the core workshop)
             - Live code on the projector
             - Attendees follow along on their laptops
             - Pause every 5-7 minutes to let people catch up
             - Have 1-2 helpers roaming the room for troubleshooting

0:50 - 1:10  Free build time
             - Attendees customize and extend what they built
             - Provide 3-4 stretch challenges for fast finishers
             - Helpers continue circulating

1:10 - 1:20  Show and tell
             - 2-3 volunteers demo what they built or customized
             - Celebrate creativity and effort, not polish

1:20 - 1:30  Wrap-up
             - What is coming next week (create anticipation)
             - How to join the Discord / mailing list
             - Collect feedback (short form or verbal)
```

:::callout[warning]
The most common first-event mistake is trying to cover too much. If you are debating between "teach one thing well" and "give a broad overview," always choose depth over breadth. A student who builds one working chatbot remembers the experience. A student who hears about five AI topics remembers none of them.
:::

## Handling Mixed Skill Levels

Your first event will have complete beginners sitting next to students who have shipped production ML models. This is normal and manageable.

### The layered approach

Design your workshop with three tiers:

1. **Baseline (everyone completes this).** The guided portion that requires no prior knowledge. Following along with the live coding gets you to a working result.
2. **Extensions (most people attempt one).** 3-4 additional features or modifications listed on a slide or handout. "Now try adding X" or "Can you make it do Y?"
3. **Stretch goals (advanced students pursue these).** Open-ended challenges that require independent problem-solving. "Build a completely different application using the same API."

This way, beginners have a clear path to success, and experienced students are never bored.

### Helper strategy

Recruit 2-3 helpers for your first event. These can be your co-founders or technically strong members from your interest form. Their job:

- Circulate the room during the guided build
- Help people who fall behind (without taking over their keyboard)
- Sit next to anyone who looks lost or frustrated
- Never say "that is easy" or "you just need to..."

:::callout[info]
Brief your helpers before the event. The most important instruction: "If someone is stuck, ask them what they have tried before jumping in with the answer." Teaching people to debug is more valuable than fixing their code for them.
:::

## Pre-Event Logistics

A smooth first event requires preparation. Do not wing it.

### One week before

- [ ] Confirm room booking and check the AV setup (projector, screen, power outlets)
- [ ] Test your workshop end-to-end on a clean machine (not your development setup)
- [ ] Write setup instructions and send them to registered attendees ("Install X, create an account at Y, bring your laptop")
- [ ] Create a sign-in sheet or digital check-in (Google Form with name and email)
- [ ] Prepare a backup plan for WiFi failure (downloaded dependencies, offline-capable exercises)
- [ ] Post reminders on all your channels (Discord, Instagram, email)

### Day of the event

- [ ] Arrive 20 minutes early to set up
- [ ] Test the projector and WiFi
- [ ] Put the WiFi password and setup instructions on the projector screen before people arrive
- [ ] Have the Discord/Slack join link displayed prominently (QR code on a slide works great)
- [ ] Set out a sign-in sheet or have a QR code for digital check-in at the door

### Common first-event failures and how to prevent them

| Failure | Prevention |
|---|---|
| WiFi crashes with 30 people connected | Have a hotspot backup; pre-download large dependencies |
| Setup takes 30 minutes and kills momentum | Send setup instructions in advance; have a "setup help desk" running at the start |
| Content is too hard for beginners | Test with a non-technical friend beforehand |
| Content is too easy for advanced students | Prepare stretch goals and extensions |
| Nobody asks questions | Seed questions with your co-founders; use "turn to your neighbor" prompts |
| Low attendance (5-8 people) | This is fine. A great experience for 5 people beats a mediocre one for 30 |

## After the Event

What you do in the 24 hours after your first event matters as much as the event itself.

### Immediately after

- [ ] Thank everyone who came (verbally and in a follow-up message)
- [ ] Send a recap message to your Discord/email list with: what you built, links to resources, and what is coming next week
- [ ] Share any photos on social media (with permission)

### Within 48 hours

- [ ] Review sign-in data -- how many people came versus how many signed up?
- [ ] Read through feedback (if you collected it)
- [ ] Debrief with your co-founders: What worked? What did not? What do you change for next time?
- [ ] Personally message 3-5 attendees who seemed engaged and ask what they thought

:::callout[tip]
The personal follow-up messages are the highest-leverage thing you can do after your first event. People who feel personally welcomed are far more likely to return. A generic "thanks for coming" email does not have the same effect as a direct message saying "Hey, I noticed you built that cool extension to the chatbot -- would love to see you next week."
:::

## First Event Checklist

- [ ] Chosen a workshop format with zero prerequisites and a tangible deliverable
- [ ] Prepared a layered workshop (baseline, extensions, stretch goals)
- [ ] Tested the entire workshop end-to-end on a clean machine
- [ ] Recruited 2-3 helpers and briefed them on their role
- [ ] Sent setup instructions to attendees in advance
- [ ] Confirmed room, AV, and WiFi
- [ ] Prepared sign-in and feedback collection
- [ ] Planned your post-event follow-up (recap message, personal outreach, social media)

Your first event does not need to be perfect. It needs to be genuine, well-prepared, and leave people feeling like they accomplished something. Everything else you can iterate on.
