# Finding Sponsors and Advisors

Industry partners and faculty advisors are force multipliers. A good sponsor provides funding, credibility, guest speakers, and career opportunities for your members. A good faculty advisor opens doors within the university that students cannot open alone. This section covers how to pitch your club to companies, what sponsors actually want in return, and how to build faculty relationships that go beyond a signature on a form.

## Why Sponsorship Matters

Let us be clear about what sponsorship enables:

- **Food at events.** This sounds trivial, but free pizza increases attendance by 30-50% at most campuses. It lowers the barrier to showing up.
- **Cloud credits and API access.** AI workshops require API calls, compute, and cloud resources. Sponsors can cover these costs.
- **Swag and prizes.** Stickers, t-shirts, and gift cards for hackathons and competitions make events more memorable.
- **Guest speakers.** Engineers and leaders from sponsor companies can run workshops, give talks, and mentor members.
- **Hiring pipeline.** Sponsors recruit from your club. Your members get internships and jobs. This is the value exchange that sustains long-term partnerships.

:::callout[info]
You do not need sponsors to launch. Your first 2-3 events can run on zero budget. But by mid-semester, having at least one sponsor dramatically expands what you can do and signals to members that the club is serious.
:::

## What Sponsors Want

Before you pitch anyone, understand their motivations. Companies sponsor student organizations for three reasons:

1. **Talent pipeline.** They want to hire your members. This is the primary driver for most tech company sponsorships.
2. **Brand awareness.** They want students to know their products, tools, or platform. Developer relations teams have budgets specifically for this.
3. **Community goodwill.** Some companies (especially local ones) sponsor student orgs because they genuinely want to support education in their region.

Your pitch should address at least one of these directly. "We need money for pizza" is not a pitch. "We have 30 students learning applied AI every week and we can connect your company with top talent" is.

## Identifying Potential Sponsors

### Tier 1: AI and developer tools companies

These companies have established developer relations teams with budgets for exactly this kind of sponsorship. They are the most likely to say yes and the easiest to pitch.

Look for companies that offer:

- AI APIs or platforms (language model providers, cloud AI services)
- Developer tools (IDEs, deployment platforms, databases)
- Cloud computing (credits programs for student organizations)
- AI infrastructure (GPU providers, MLOps platforms)

Many of these companies have formal programs for student organizations. Check their websites for "education," "community," or "developer programs" pages.

### Tier 2: Local and regional tech companies

Companies in your university's metro area are excellent sponsors because:

- They actively recruit from your school
- They want local visibility
- They are easier to build a personal relationship with
- They are often more flexible and responsive than large corporations

### Tier 3: Non-tech companies with AI initiatives

Banks, healthcare companies, retailers, and manufacturers are all investing heavily in AI. Their innovation or R&D teams may be interested in sponsoring a student AI community.

:::callout[tip]
Your ALL Applied AI Network affiliation is a significant asset when pitching sponsors. You are not a random new club -- you are part of a national network with a proven model, established curriculum, and a track record of producing talent. Use it.
:::

## Crafting the Pitch

### The cold outreach email

Most sponsorship conversations start with an email. Here is a structure that works:

```
Subject: Partnership with [University] Applied AI Hub — [X] students building with AI weekly

Hi [name],

I am [your name], the founder of [club name] at [university]. We are a
community of [X] students who build applied AI projects every week --
from chatbots to RAG systems to production AI applications. We are
part of the ALL Applied AI Network, a national network of university
AI communities.

I am reaching out because [specific reason you are contacting them --
their product is relevant, they have a presence in your area, you saw
they sponsor student events, etc.].

We are looking for partners who want to:
- Connect with [X] students actively learning AI development
- Have their tools and platform used in hands-on workshops
- Access a pipeline of talented students for internships and hiring

In return, we offer:
- Logo placement on our materials and event promotions
- A workshop or talk slot for your team to present to our members
- Direct introductions to our most engaged members for recruiting
- Social media coverage of sponsored events

Our next event is [date]. Would you be open to a 15-minute call to
discuss how we could work together?

Best,
[Your name]
[Your role], [Club name]
[University]
[Email] | [LinkedIn]
```

### What to include in a sponsorship deck

If a company asks for more information, have a one-page or short slide deck ready:

- [ ] Club overview: mission, size, meeting frequency
- [ ] Member demographics: majors, year distribution, skill levels
- [ ] Event formats: what a typical meeting looks like
- [ ] Past highlights: photos, project demos, attendance numbers
- [ ] Sponsorship tiers: what you offer at different levels
- [ ] The ask: specific amount or resources you are requesting

### Sponsorship tiers

Structure your ask in tiers so sponsors can choose their level of involvement:

| Tier | Typical value | What the sponsor provides | What you provide |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Community Partner** | $0-250 / semester | Swag, stickers, small prizes | Logo on website, social media mention |
| **Event Sponsor** | $250-500 / event | Food for an event, API credits | Co-branded event, sponsor intro at meeting, recruiting access |
| **Semester Partner** | $500-2000 / semester | Recurring funding, cloud credits, equipment | Logo on all materials, dedicated workshop slot, priority recruiting access, member introductions |
| **Strategic Partner** | $2000+ / semester | Major funding, equipment, mentorship | Deep integration -- co-developed curriculum, exclusive recruiting events, advisory board seat |

:::callout[warning]
Do not undersell yourself. A room full of 30 students actively learning AI is extremely valuable to companies. A single good hire is worth tens of thousands of dollars to them. Your sponsorship pricing should reflect the genuine value you provide.
:::

## Building Faculty Relationships

Your faculty advisor relationship can range from "they signed a form and you never talk" to "they actively champion your club and connect you with industry." Aim for the latter.

### Beyond the signature

A great faculty advisor:

- Introduces you to their industry contacts
- Lets you announce events in their classes
- Provides academic credibility when you approach sponsors
- Advocates for your club in faculty meetings and department discussions
- Offers mentorship on project ideas and technical content
- Connects you with graduate students who can help run advanced workshops

### How to build a strong advisor relationship

1. **Meet regularly.** A 15-minute check-in every 2-3 weeks is enough. Update them on what is happening, ask for advice on specific decisions, and share wins.
2. **Invite them to events.** Not every event, but key ones: the first meeting, the end-of-semester showcase, guest speaker events.
3. **Ask for specific help.** "Can you introduce me to someone at [company]?" is better than "Do you know anyone who might sponsor us?"
4. **Make them look good.** When your club succeeds, your advisor gets credit within the department. Share metrics, photos, and member testimonials that they can include in their own reports.
5. **Respect their time.** Faculty are busy. Be organized, concise, and grateful.

### Building a faculty network (beyond your advisor)

One advisor is the minimum. Over time, build relationships with multiple faculty:

- [ ] Professors who teach AI-related courses -- potential workshop collaborators
- [ ] Department chairs -- can allocate resources and advocate for your club
- [ ] Career services staff -- can connect you with employer partners
- [ ] Research faculty -- potential mentors for advanced project teams

:::callout[tip]
Invite a professor to give a 15-minute talk at one of your meetings about their research. Faculty love talking about their work to an engaged audience, and it builds a relationship that leads to deeper collaboration. It also gives your members exposure to the academic side of AI.
:::

## Managing Sponsor Relationships

Landing a sponsor is the beginning, not the end. Keeping sponsors requires consistent communication and delivered value.

### The sponsor relationship cadence

| When | What to do |
|---|---|
| After agreement | Send a thank-you email with a timeline of deliverables |
| Before each sponsored event | Confirm logistics, share the event plan, ask if they want to send a representative |
| After each sponsored event | Send a recap with attendance numbers, photos, and member feedback |
| Monthly | Quick update email with club metrics and highlights |
| End of semester | Comprehensive recap with outcomes, photos, and a pitch for continuing next semester |

### What to do when things go wrong

- **Low attendance at a sponsored event:** Be honest. Share the numbers, explain the context (midterms, weather), and outline what you will do differently next time.
- **A sponsor asks for something you cannot deliver:** Say no clearly and offer an alternative. "We cannot guarantee 50 attendees, but we can guarantee 20 highly engaged students who chose to be here."
- **A sponsor ghosts you:** Follow up twice, then move on. Do not chase sponsors who are not responsive.

## Sponsorship and Advisor Checklist

- [ ] Identified at least 5 potential sponsors across the three tiers
- [ ] Drafted a cold outreach email template customized for your club
- [ ] Created a one-page sponsorship deck with tiers and deliverables
- [ ] Sent outreach to at least 3 companies
- [ ] Scheduled regular check-ins with your faculty advisor
- [ ] Invited your advisor to at least one event this semester
- [ ] Set up a system for tracking sponsor communications and deliverables
- [ ] Planned your end-of-semester sponsor recap

Sponsorships and advisor relationships are long-term investments. The first conversation rarely closes a deal. Be patient, deliver on what you promise, and build trust over multiple semesters. A strong sponsor relationship that lasts three years is worth far more than a dozen one-time donations.
