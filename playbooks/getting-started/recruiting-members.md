# Recruiting Members

Flyers in the CS building are not enough. Recruiting for a student org is a multi-channel effort, and the channels that work best are probably not the ones you expect. This section covers how to fill your room consistently, how to reach students outside of engineering, and how to turn first-time attendees into regulars.

## The Recruiting Funnel

Think of recruiting as a funnel with four stages. Your job is to optimize each transition, not just the top.

```
Awareness       ->  Someone hears your club exists
Interest        ->  They check you out (social media, website, friend's recommendation)
First visit     ->  They show up to a meeting
Retention       ->  They come back a second and third time
```

Most clubs pour all their energy into awareness (posting flyers, making announcements) and ignore the rest. But the conversion from first visit to retention is where clubs live or die. A hundred people hearing about you means nothing if only three come back after their first meeting.

## Channel-by-Channel Strategy

### Student org fairs

This is your single highest-ROI recruiting opportunity. Most universities hold an org fair at the start of each semester where hundreds of students browse tables.

**How to stand out:**

- [ ] Have a live demo running on a laptop at your table (a chatbot, an AI art generator, something visual and interactive)
- [ ] Let visitors try it themselves -- do not just show a slideshow
- [ ] Collect emails on a tablet or phone (QR code to a Google Form), not a paper sign-up sheet (paper gets lost)
- [ ] Have your one-liner ready: "We are [club name] -- we build real AI projects every week. No experience needed."
- [ ] Bring 2-3 team members so someone is always engaging visitors while others handle sign-ups

:::callout[tip]
The live demo is the single most effective thing you can do at an org fair. Students walk past dozens of tables with banners and candy. A table where they can talk to an AI chatbot or generate an image stops them in their tracks.
:::

### Class announcements

Professors will often give you 60-90 seconds at the start of class to promote your club. This is gold -- you are speaking to a captive, relevant audience.

**How to make the most of it:**

1. Email the professor a week in advance asking for permission. Include a one-sentence description of your club.
2. Keep your pitch under 60 seconds. State what the club is, when and where you meet, and one specific thing you are building next week.
3. Put a QR code on the projector that links to your Discord or interest form.
4. Do not read from a script. Be genuine and enthusiastic.

**Which classes to target:**

- Intro to CS / Programming 101 (large audience, many undecided students)
- Data structures / Algorithms (students actively building programming skills)
- Any AI/ML course (obvious alignment)
- Statistics and data analysis courses (adjacent interest)
- Business analytics or information systems (non-CS students who use AI tools)
- Any large general-education course (for breadth)

:::callout[info]
Do not limit yourself to CS courses. Some of the most engaged members at established hubs come from business, biology, psychology, and design programs. AI is cross-disciplinary -- your recruiting should be too.
:::

### Social media

Instagram is the primary discovery platform for college students finding clubs. LinkedIn matters for credibility with sponsors and faculty but is secondary for member recruiting.

**Instagram strategy:**

- Post 2-3 times per week during active recruiting periods
- Content types that perform well: event announcements with dates/times, photos from meetings (real, not staged), short clips of demos or projects, member spotlights, "what we built this week" posts
- Use your university's hashtags and location tags
- Stories are more important than feed posts for engagement -- use polls, questions, and countdowns for events

**What not to do on social media:**

- Do not post generic AI news articles. Your members can find those themselves.
- Do not create content that looks like a corporate brand. Authenticity wins.
- Do not neglect it for weeks and then post a flurry of content. Consistency matters more than volume.

### Word of mouth

This is your most powerful channel, but you cannot force it. You earn word of mouth by running events that people talk about afterward.

**How to catalyze word of mouth:**

- At the end of every meeting, say: "If you had a good time tonight, bring a friend next week."
- Create shareable moments: group photos, project demos, competitions with results people want to post about.
- Ask engaged members to personally invite one friend. A personal invitation from a peer is 10x more effective than any flyer.

### Department and university channels

Many channels exist that clubs underutilize:

- [ ] Department email newsletters (ask the department admin to include your events)
- [ ] University event calendars (submit your meetings as recurring events)
- [ ] Digital signage in department buildings (many universities have screens in hallways that display student org events)
- [ ] Residence hall bulletin boards and newsletters
- [ ] Other clubs' Discord servers or Slack workspaces (ask permission first, do not spam)

## Recruiting Beyond Engineering

One of the biggest growth opportunities is reaching students outside of CS and engineering. AI tools are relevant to every field, and these students are often the most enthusiastic because they have not been exposed to AI in their coursework.

### Why non-technical students matter

- They bring diverse perspectives and problem domains
- They are often the most creative users of AI tools
- They expand your member base beyond a single department
- They make your club more interesting to sponsors (companies want interdisciplinary talent)

### How to reach them

1. **Frame your marketing around outcomes, not tools.** "Build an AI app" speaks to CS students. "Use AI to automate your research" or "Build tools that solve real problems" speaks to everyone.
2. **Partner with non-technical clubs.** Run a joint event with the entrepreneurship club, the pre-med society, or the design club. Show them what AI can do for their domain.
3. **Explicitly state "no experience required"** in every piece of marketing. Say it early and often. Non-technical students self-select out if they sense any gatekeeping.
4. **Feature non-CS members in your marketing.** When a business student builds something cool at your workshop, spotlight them. Representation signals welcome.

:::callout[warning]
If your first few events are pitched as "intermediate Python" or use heavy jargon in the description, non-technical students will never show up. Use plain language in your event descriptions and save the technical depth for the event itself, where you can scaffold it properly.
:::

## Converting First-Timers to Regulars

Getting someone to show up once is the easy part. Getting them back is the challenge.

### The first 5 minutes matter most

When a new person walks in, they are evaluating whether they belong here. Within the first 5 minutes, they should:

- Be greeted by name (use your sign-in sheet from last time, or introduce yourself immediately)
- Know where to sit and what to do
- See at least one person who looks like them (in skill level, major, or background)
- Understand what is happening today and what they will get out of it

### The follow-up formula

After someone attends for the first time:

1. **Within 24 hours:** Send a personal message (not a mass email) thanking them for coming and asking what they thought.
2. **3 days before the next meeting:** Send a reminder about what is happening next week and why it builds on what they just did.
3. **At the next meeting:** Greet them by name and reference something from last time. ("Hey, how did that chatbot extension go?")

### Why people do not come back (and what to do about it)

| Reason | Solution |
|---|---|
| "I felt lost the whole time" | Better scaffolding, more helpers, explicit "no experience needed" messaging |
| "It was boring" | More hands-on time, less talking, better content selection |
| "I did not know anyone" | Structured icebreakers, small-group activities, deliberate introductions |
| "I forgot about it" | Consistent reminders through Discord and email |
| "The time does not work" | Survey your members and pick the time that works for the most people |
| "I did not see the point" | Clearer value proposition, more visible outcomes (projects, portfolios, jobs) |

## Tracking Recruiting Metrics

You cannot improve what you do not measure. Track these numbers each week:

- **Sign-in count:** How many people attended?
- **New vs. returning:** How many are first-timers versus repeat attendees?
- **Retention rate:** What percentage of last week's attendees came back this week?
- **Source:** How did new members hear about you? (Add this to your sign-in form)

:::callout[tip]
A simple spreadsheet is all you need. Track weekly attendance and tag each person as new or returning. After a month, you will see clear patterns in what is working and what is not.
:::

### Healthy benchmarks for a new club

| Metric | First month | By end of semester |
|---|---|---|
| Average attendance | 10-15 | 20-30 |
| Retention (week over week) | 40-50% | 60-70% |
| New members per week | 3-5 | 1-3 (and that is fine) |
| Active Discord members | 20-30 | 50-80 |

These numbers are guidelines, not mandates. A club with 12 highly engaged members who build real projects is healthier than a club with 40 names on a list and 8 people who actually show up.

## Recruiting Checklist

- [ ] Registered for the student org fair and prepared your table (live demo, sign-up form, team members)
- [ ] Scheduled class announcements in at least 5 relevant courses
- [ ] Set up and started posting on Instagram with a consistent cadence
- [ ] Submitted events to university calendars and department newsletters
- [ ] Made at least one outreach effort to non-engineering students
- [ ] Created a sign-in system that tracks new vs. returning members
- [ ] Established a personal follow-up process for first-time attendees
- [ ] Set up a weekly attendance tracker

Recruiting is not a one-time push at the start of the semester. It is a sustained effort that gets easier as your reputation builds. The best recruiting tool you have is a club that people genuinely enjoy attending -- everything else is just making sure they know it exists.
