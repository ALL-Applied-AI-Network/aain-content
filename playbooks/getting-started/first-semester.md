# Running Your First Semester

You have a team, a registered org, and a successful first event. Now you need to sustain it for 12-15 weeks. This section provides a week-by-week framework, covers content planning, teaches you how to handle the inevitable low-attendance weeks, and tells you when to start delegating so you do not burn out.

## The Semester at a Glance

Your first semester follows a natural arc. Plan for it instead of being surprised by it.

```
Weeks 1-3:    Launch phase      High energy, high attendance, everything feels exciting
Weeks 4-6:    Settling in       Attendance stabilizes, casual attendees drop off
Weeks 7-9:    The mid-semester dip  Midterms hit, energy drops, this is normal
Weeks 10-12:  Second wind       Post-midterm recovery, project momentum builds
Weeks 13-15:  Strong close      Showcase, end-of-semester event, set up next semester
```

:::callout[info]
The mid-semester dip is not a failure. Every student org experiences it. Plan lighter content during midterm weeks and do not panic when attendance drops by 30-40%. The students who come back after the dip are your core community.
:::

## Choosing Your Meeting Cadence

Weekly meetings are the standard, and for good reason. Less frequent than weekly and people forget you exist. More frequent and you burn out your team and exhaust your content pipeline.

**Recommended schedule:**

- **Weekly meetings:** Your primary event. Same day, same time, same room, every week. Consistency is more important than finding the "perfect" time.
- **Office hours (optional):** A casual 1-2 hour block where members can drop in for help with projects. Low effort to run, high value for engaged members.
- **Monthly special event:** One meeting per month that breaks the routine -- a guest speaker, a hackathon, a social event, or a demo night.

:::callout[tip]
Pick a meeting day early in the week (Tuesday or Wednesday). Monday has holiday conflicts. Thursday and Friday compete with social plans. Survey your founding members if you are unsure, but make a decision quickly and stick with it.
:::

## Content Planning

Running out of content ideas by week 5 is one of the most common first-semester failures. Plan your content calendar before the semester starts.

### The content mix

A healthy semester includes three types of content in rotation:

| Content type | Frequency | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Workshops** | Every 2-3 weeks | Guided, hands-on sessions where everyone builds something |
| **Build nights** | Every 2-3 weeks | Less structured time for members to work on projects with support available |
| **Special events** | Monthly | Guest speakers, demo nights, competitions, or social events |

### Sample 14-week content calendar

| Week | Type | Topic |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Workshop | Kickoff: Build a chatbot with an AI API |
| 2 | Workshop | Prompt engineering: getting better outputs from AI models |
| 3 | Build night | Open project time -- extend what you built in weeks 1-2 |
| 4 | Workshop | Introduction to AI IDEs: building with Cursor |
| 5 | Special | Guest speaker: industry professional on applied AI careers |
| 6 | Workshop | Building a RAG application from scratch |
| 7 | Build night | Midterm week -- light session, project work time |
| 8 | Workshop | Deploying your AI app so others can use it |
| 9 | Build night | Team project kickoff -- form teams, choose projects |
| 10 | Workshop | AI agents: giving your application the ability to take actions |
| 11 | Build night | Team project work session |
| 12 | Special | Mini-hackathon: 3-hour build sprint with prizes |
| 13 | Build night | Team project polish and prep for showcase |
| 14 | Special | End-of-semester showcase and social |

:::callout[tip]
You do not need to create all workshop content from scratch. The ALL Applied AI Network learning tree and workshop library provide tested content you can run directly or adapt. Focus your original content creation on topics specific to your campus or members' interests.
:::

### How to plan workshop content

For each workshop, define these elements at least one week in advance:

1. **Learning objective:** One sentence describing what attendees walk away knowing how to do
2. **Prerequisites:** What do attendees need installed or set up before arriving?
3. **Time breakdown:** How many minutes for intro, guided build, free build, and wrap-up?
4. **Materials:** Starter code, slides, handouts, or links
5. **Stretch goals:** What do fast finishers work on?
6. **Helpers needed:** How many, and what should they know?

## Handling Low-Attendance Weeks

It will happen. You will plan a great workshop and six people show up. Here is how to handle it.

### Do not take it personally

Low attendance is usually caused by:

- Midterms or finals
- Weather (especially at northern campuses)
- Competing campus events
- A holiday weekend
- Bad luck

It is almost never because people stopped caring about your club.

### Adapt on the fly

| Planned for 25 people | Got 6 people instead |
|---|---|
| Lecture-style workshop | Switch to small-group discussion |
| Formal presentation | Informal Q&A or pair programming |
| Structured agenda | Flexible build night |

Six engaged people in a room is an opportunity for a more personal, high-quality session. Lean into it.

:::callout[warning]
The worst thing you can do during a low-attendance week is to act defeated or apologize for the turnout. The people who showed up chose to be there. Make it worth their time and they will come back -- and bring friends.
:::

### Prevent low attendance proactively

- Send reminders 24 hours before every meeting (Discord, email, Instagram story)
- Tease the specific content: "Tomorrow we are building X" is better than "Meeting tomorrow"
- If you know a low-attendance week is coming (midterms), plan a lighter session and communicate that ("Low-key build night this week -- bring your project and your questions")
- Track attendance patterns and adjust your calendar accordingly

## Delegating and Distributing Work

By week 4-5, the founding team should not be doing everything. Start distributing responsibility to engaged members.

### What to delegate first

| Task | When to delegate | Who to delegate to |
|---|---|---|
| Running sign-in at the door | Week 2-3 | Any reliable member |
| Social media posts | Week 3-4 | A member who is already active on Instagram |
| Workshop setup (projector, chairs) | Week 2 | Rotate among members |
| Leading a workshop session | Week 5-6 | A technically strong member who wants to teach |
| Managing the Discord server | Week 3-4 | An active community member |

### How to delegate without losing quality

1. **Give clear ownership, not vague assignments.** "You own social media posts for the next month" is better than "Can you help with social media sometimes?"
2. **Provide templates and examples.** If someone is writing their first Instagram post, show them three good ones to model.
3. **Review before publishing (at first).** Check their work the first two times, then trust them.
4. **Thank people publicly.** Shout out contributors at the start of meetings.

:::callout[info]
Delegation is not just about reducing your workload. It is about building a pipeline of future leaders. Every member who leads a workshop, manages a channel, or runs an event is practicing the skills they need to lead the club after you graduate.
:::

## Managing Your Team's Energy

Burnout is real and it does not wait until finals week. Watch for these signs in yourself and your co-founders:

- Dreading meetings instead of looking forward to them
- Cutting corners on preparation because you are exhausted
- Snapping at teammates over small decisions
- Skipping your weekly sync

### Prevention strategies

- **Rotate heavy weeks.** If one person led this week's workshop, someone else leads next week.
- **Build in breaks.** Skip the meeting during Thanksgiving week. Give yourselves permission to take a week off if you need it.
- **Keep the weekly sync short.** 30 minutes, not 90. Use an agenda, end on time.
- **Celebrate wins.** It is easy to focus on what went wrong. Take 2 minutes at the start of each sync to name something that went well.

## Mid-Semester Check-In

Around week 6-7, do a structured check-in with your founding team. Review these questions:

- [ ] Are we hitting our attendance targets? If not, why?
- [ ] Which event formats are working best? Which should we drop?
- [ ] Is the workload distributed fairly across the team?
- [ ] Are any co-founders showing signs of burnout?
- [ ] What feedback are we hearing from members?
- [ ] Do we need to adjust our content calendar for the second half?
- [ ] Are we on track to have a strong end-of-semester event?

### Gathering member feedback

Send a short (5-question max) survey to your members around week 6:

1. What has been your favorite meeting so far, and why?
2. What would you like to see more of?
3. Is the difficulty level right? (Too easy / Just right / Too hard)
4. What day and time works best for you?
5. Would you recommend this club to a friend? (Yes / Maybe / No) -- and why or why not?

## Planning the End-of-Semester Showcase

Your last meeting of the semester should be memorable. An end-of-semester showcase serves multiple purposes:

- Gives members a deadline to finish projects
- Creates content for social media and recruiting
- Demonstrates value to sponsors and faculty
- Builds momentum going into the next semester

### Showcase format

- 5-7 minute demo slots for teams or individuals
- A panel of 2-3 judges (faculty advisor, industry partner, senior member)
- Simple prizes (gift cards, swag, or just recognition)
- Food and social time afterward
- Invite your faculty advisor, department contacts, and any sponsors

:::callout[tip]
Take photos and video at the showcase. This content is invaluable for recruiting next semester and for showing sponsors what their support enables. Assign someone to be the dedicated photographer for the event.
:::

## First Semester Checklist

- [ ] Meeting cadence established (weekly meetings, same day/time/place)
- [ ] Content calendar planned for the full semester
- [ ] Reminders going out 24 hours before every meeting
- [ ] Attendance tracked weekly (new vs. returning members)
- [ ] Delegation started by week 4-5
- [ ] Mid-semester check-in completed (team review and member survey)
- [ ] End-of-semester showcase planned and promoted
- [ ] Next semester's leadership pipeline identified (who could step into bigger roles?)

Your first semester will not be perfect. Events will flop, attendance will fluctuate, and you will make mistakes you did not anticipate. That is all normal. What matters is that you showed up every week, delivered value to your members, and built something that people want to continue.
