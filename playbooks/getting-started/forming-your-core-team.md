# Forming Your Core Team

You cannot build a sustainable club alone. Even if you are the most motivated person on campus, solo founders burn out, create single points of failure, and miss blind spots. This section covers how to recruit 2-4 co-founders, define roles without drowning in bureaucracy, and build a working rhythm before you ever go public.

## How Many Co-Founders Do You Need?

The sweet spot is 3-5 people total, including you. Here is why:

- **2 people** works but is fragile. If one person gets sick, has a bad week, or drops out, the club dies.
- **3-4 people** is ideal. Enough to distribute work, diverse enough to cover different skills, small enough to make decisions fast.
- **5+ people** before you have even launched is usually a sign that you are building a committee, not a team. Keep it tight at first.

:::callout[tip]
You are not looking for people who are "interested in AI." You are looking for people who will show up every week, do unglamorous work (booking rooms, making flyers, writing emails), and stick around when attendance dips. Reliability beats expertise every time.
:::

## What Skills to Look For

A strong founding team covers four areas. One person can cover multiple areas, but every area needs at least one owner.

| Role | What they do | Skills to look for |
|---|---|---|
| **Technical Lead** | Designs workshops, writes tutorials, troubleshoots during events | Strong programming skills, can explain concepts clearly |
| **Operations Lead** | Handles logistics -- rooms, forms, budgets, university paperwork | Organized, follows through, comfortable with bureaucracy |
| **Community Lead** | Manages social media, sends emails, talks to people at events | Outgoing, good writer, active on relevant platforms |
| **Partnerships Lead** | Reaches out to faculty, companies, and other organizations | Professional communication skills, not afraid of cold emails |

### A note on technical depth

Your Technical Lead does not need to be an AI researcher. They need to be someone who can learn a new tool in a weekend, build a working demo, and walk a room full of beginners through it. Applied AI skills matter more than academic credentials here.

:::callout[warning]
Avoid building a team where everyone has the same skill set. Four strong programmers with no one willing to handle logistics or outreach is a recipe for a club that builds cool things but nobody knows about.
:::

## Where to Find Co-Founders

Your co-founders are probably already in your network, even if you do not realize it yet.

### High-yield places to look

- **Your validation respondents.** Go back to the interest form from the previous section. Who wrote enthusiastic responses? Who followed up unprompted?
- **Study groups and project teams.** People you have already worked well with under pressure.
- **Adjacent clubs.** Members of CS, data science, or engineering clubs who seem restless or under-challenged.
- **Class standouts.** Students who build beyond what the assignment requires.
- **TAs and lab assistants.** They already know how to explain technical concepts to peers.

### The pitch

Be direct. Here is a template for reaching out:

> "Hey [name], I am starting an applied AI club on campus -- a community where students build real AI projects every week. I have validated interest from [X] students and I am looking for 2-3 co-founders to help launch it this semester. I think your [specific skill] would be a huge asset. Want to grab coffee and talk about it?"

Notice what this pitch does:

1. States the vision concretely ("build real AI projects every week")
2. Shows you have already done legwork ("validated interest from X students")
3. Explains why them specifically ("your specific skill")
4. Low-commitment ask ("grab coffee")

## Define Roles Without Creating Bureaucracy

Student organizations have a tendency to over-formalize early. You do not need a constitution with six officer positions, a parliamentary procedure, or Robert's Rules of Order. You need clear ownership of tasks.

### The responsibility matrix

Create a simple document (a shared Google Doc or Notion page works) that maps who owns what:

```
FOUNDING TEAM RESPONSIBILITIES
Last updated: [date]

[Name 1] - Technical Lead
- Designs workshop content for weekly meetings
- Maintains the club GitHub organization
- Reviews technical content before events

[Name 2] - Operations Lead
- Books rooms for all events
- Manages the club email account
- Handles university registration paperwork
- Tracks budget and expenses

[Name 3] - Community & Partnerships
- Runs social media accounts (Instagram, LinkedIn)
- Sends weekly email updates to members
- Reaches out to potential sponsors and speakers
- Coordinates with other student organizations
```

:::callout[info]
Titles are for internal clarity, not for impressing people. What matters is that every recurring task has exactly one owner. If something does not have an owner, it will not get done.
:::

### Decision-making protocol

Agree on how you make decisions before you disagree on anything. For a founding team of 3-4, this works well:

1. **Day-to-day decisions** (room choice, social media posts, email wording): The owner of that area decides. No group approval needed.
2. **Strategic decisions** (event format changes, partnership agreements, spending over $50): Discuss as a team, majority rules.
3. **Existential decisions** (changing the club's mission, removing a co-founder): Unanimous agreement required.

Write this down. It sounds excessive now, but you will be grateful for it during your first real disagreement.

## Establish a Working Rhythm

Before you recruit general members or host public events, your core team needs to build habits together.

### The weekly sync

Set a standing 30-minute meeting with your co-founders. Same time, same place, every week. Use this agenda:

1. **Check-in** (2 min): How is everyone doing? Any conflicts with upcoming deadlines?
2. **Progress updates** (10 min): What did each person accomplish since last meeting?
3. **Blockers** (5 min): What is stuck? Who needs help?
4. **Next week's priorities** (10 min): What does each person commit to completing?
5. **Quick decisions** (3 min): Any decisions that need group input?

:::callout[tip]
Keep meeting notes in a shared document. Even rough bullet points. This creates an institutional record that will matter when you eventually hand leadership to the next generation.
:::

### Communication norms

Agree on these upfront:

- **Primary channel:** Where does the team communicate day-to-day? (Discord server, group chat, Slack workspace)
- **Response time:** What is a reasonable response time for messages? (24 hours for non-urgent, 2 hours for event-day issues)
- **Document storage:** Where do shared files live? (Google Drive folder, Notion workspace, GitHub repo)

## Set Shared Expectations

The number one reason founding teams fracture is mismatched expectations. Have this conversation early, even if it feels awkward.

### Questions every co-founder should answer

1. How many hours per week can you realistically commit?
2. What is your biggest motivator for doing this? (Learning, community, resume, fun)
3. What would make you want to quit?
4. Are there weeks this semester where you will be unavailable? (Exam weeks, travel, jobs)
5. What does success look like to you at the end of this semester?

Write the answers down. If someone says they can commit 2 hours per week and the role requires 5, address that now -- not in week 6 when resentment has built up.

### The co-founder agreement

This is not a legal document. It is a one-page shared understanding. Include:

- [ ] Each person's role and key responsibilities
- [ ] Expected weekly time commitment
- [ ] How decisions get made
- [ ] What happens if someone needs to step back
- [ ] When you will revisit and update this agreement (suggest: end of first month)

## Red Flags in Potential Co-Founders

Watch for these patterns during your initial conversations:

- **"I have a ton of ideas."** Ideas are cheap. Ask what they have actually executed on.
- **"I am really busy but I will make it work."** If they are overcommitted now, adding a club will not help.
- **"I want to be president."** Title-seekers rarely do the work. Look for people who want to build, not people who want a line on their resume.
- **"Let me know what you need me to do."** You need self-starters, not task-followers. Co-founders identify problems and solve them without being asked.

## Core Team Checklist

Before moving to campus registration, confirm:

- [ ] You have 2-4 co-founders committed (3-5 total including you)
- [ ] Each person has a defined role with specific responsibilities
- [ ] You have agreed on a decision-making process
- [ ] You have a standing weekly sync on the calendar
- [ ] You have chosen your communication tools and document storage
- [ ] Each co-founder has stated their time commitment and expectations
- [ ] You have a written (even informal) co-founder agreement

With your team in place, you are ready to make it official.
