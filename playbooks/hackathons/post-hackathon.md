# Post-Hackathon

The event is over, but your work is not. The 48 hours after a hackathon are when you capture value, build relationships, and set yourself up for an even better event next time. Do not let the post-event energy fade without acting on it.

## Immediate (Within 24 Hours)

### Collect Submissions
- [ ] Close the submission platform at the deadline (no exceptions, it is not fair to teams that submitted on time)
- [ ] Verify all submissions are accessible (links work, repos are public, demo videos play)
- [ ] Export a list of all submitted projects with team names and members

### Announce Winners
- [ ] If you did not announce winners at the closing ceremony, announce them within 24 hours via email and Discord
- [ ] Post winners on social media with project descriptions and team photos
- [ ] Tag sponsor companies in winner announcements (they love this)

### Distribute Prizes
- [ ] Hand out physical prizes on event day if possible
- [ ] Send digital prizes (gift cards, credits) within 24 hours via email
- [ ] For prizes from sponsors, coordinate with the sponsor contact to ensure delivery

### Send Thank-Yous
- [ ] **Participants:** Mass email thanking everyone for attending, linking to the project showcase, and sharing the feedback survey
- [ ] **Sponsors:** Personal email to each sponsor contact with attendance numbers, photos, and social media links
- [ ] **Mentors:** Personal email thanking them for their time and asking if they would mentor again
- [ ] **Judges:** Personal email with the final results and their impact on the event
- [ ] **Venue contact:** Thank them and flag any issues that need attention

## Within One Week

### Publish a Project Showcase
Create a public page or post highlighting submitted projects. For each project include:
- Project name and one-line description
- Team member names
- Link to repo, demo, or video
- Screenshot or demo GIF

**Where to publish:**
- Your club's website
- A Devpost event page
- A social media thread (Twitter/X or LinkedIn work well)
- The ALL Applied AI Network site (coordinate with network leadership)

**Why this matters:** Participants can share this on their resumes and portfolios. It is one of the most valuable things you can give them beyond the experience itself.

### Collect Feedback
Send a short survey (5-7 questions max) to all participants:

1. Overall, how would you rate the hackathon? (1-5 stars)
2. What was the best part of the event?
3. What would you improve for next time?
4. How was the food? (this always gets honest feedback)
5. How was the mentor support?
6. Would you attend again? Would you recommend this to a friend?
7. Any other comments?

**Tip:** Send the survey in the thank-you email while the event is fresh. Response rates drop sharply after 3 days.

### Social Media Recap
Post a recap thread or carousel with:
- Attendance numbers
- Number of projects submitted
- Winning projects with descriptions
- Best photos from the event
- Quotes from participants (ask permission first)
- Thank-you to sponsors with tags

### Update Your Records
- [ ] Final budget reconciliation (total spent vs. budget, revenue from sponsors)
- [ ] Final attendance numbers (registered vs. showed up vs. stayed until the end)
- [ ] Export and archive all registration data, submissions, and feedback
- [ ] Update your sponsor tracking spreadsheet with outcomes and notes for next time

## Within Two Weeks

### Organizer Retrospective

Hold a 45-60 minute meeting with the full organizing team. This is the single most valuable thing you can do for your next event.

**Structure:**
1. **What went well (15 min):** Go around the room. Each person shares 1-2 things that worked.
2. **What did not go well (15 min):** Go around the room. Be honest. No blame, just facts.
3. **What would we do differently (15 min):** Concrete changes for next time.
4. **Action items (15 min):** Assign owners and deadlines for follow-ups.

**Common retrospective findings:**
- "We needed more power strips" (always)
- "The schedule was too tight / too loose between [specific events]"
- "Food arrived late because we ordered too late"
- "Registration over-counted -- too many no-shows"
- "We needed more mentors in the first few hours"

Write up the notes and save them where the next organizing team can find them.

### Follow Up with Sponsors
- Send each sponsor a post-event report:
  - Attendance and demographics
  - Number of projects using their platform (if applicable)
  - Photos of their branding, recruiting table, etc.
  - Social media reach and engagement
  - Participant feedback highlights
- Ask if they are interested in sponsoring next semester
- Connect them with winning teams if they expressed recruiting interest

### Continue the Projects
The best hackathon projects do not have to die after the weekend:
- Encourage winning teams to continue development
- Connect promising projects with faculty advisors for independent study credit
- Feature continued projects in future club meetings
- Submit strong projects to external hackathon showcases or competitions
- Offer follow-up workshops that help teams polish their hackathon projects (deployment, testing, UI)

## Post-Hackathon Checklist

### Day 1
- [ ] Close submissions
- [ ] Announce winners (if not done at closing)
- [ ] Distribute prizes
- [ ] Send thank-you emails (participants, sponsors, mentors, judges)
- [ ] Post initial social media recap

### Week 1
- [ ] Publish project showcase
- [ ] Send feedback survey
- [ ] Post detailed social media recap
- [ ] Reconcile budget

### Week 2
- [ ] Hold organizer retrospective
- [ ] Send sponsor post-event reports
- [ ] Write up retrospective notes for future reference
- [ ] Follow up with teams on continuing projects

### Ongoing
- [ ] Share retrospective findings with club leadership
- [ ] Begin planning timeline for next hackathon (if recurring)
- [ ] Maintain sponsor relationships with periodic updates

## Building Toward Next Time

Every hackathon you run should be better than the last. The pattern is:

1. Run the event
2. Collect feedback and hold a retrospective
3. Document what worked and what did not
4. Apply those lessons to the next event
5. Repeat

Your second hackathon will be dramatically smoother than your first. By the third, you will have a well-oiled machine. Start the planning conversation for next semester before the energy from this event fades.
