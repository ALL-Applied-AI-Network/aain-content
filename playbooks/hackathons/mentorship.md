# Mentorship and Support

Mentors are the difference between a team that gets stuck for four hours and a team that gets unstuck in twenty minutes. Good mentor support directly increases project quality and participant satisfaction. This is especially true for beginners attending their first hackathon.

## Why Mentorship Matters

- First-time hackers often do not know where to start. Mentors help them scope a realistic project.
- Even experienced teams hit walls. A 10-minute conversation with someone who has used the API before saves hours.
- Mentors model how professionals approach problems -- debugging strategies, architecture decisions, knowing when to simplify.
- Participants who interact with mentors rate their hackathon experience significantly higher.

## Recruiting Mentors

### Who Makes a Good Mentor
- Graduate students in CS, data science, or related fields
- Industry professionals (especially from sponsor companies)
- Advanced undergrads who have hackathon experience
- Faculty members willing to drop by for a few hours
- Alumni who work in relevant technical roles

### How Many Mentors
Aim for 1 mentor per 5-8 participants:
- 20-person club jam: 3-4 mentors
- 50-person hackathon: 7-10 mentors
- 100+ person event: 15-20 mentors

Not every mentor needs to be there the entire time. Staggered shifts work well.

### Where to Find Them
1. **Your own club's advanced members.** They know the vibe and the audience.
2. **Sponsor companies.** Many sponsors will send engineers as mentors -- ask this explicitly in your sponsorship conversations.
3. **CS/engineering department TAs and grad students.** They are used to helping students debug.
4. **Local tech meetup communities.** Post in Slack/Discord groups for local developers.
5. **ALL Applied AI Network.** Other hubs may have members willing to mentor virtually.

## Mentor Briefing

Hold a 15-minute briefing at the start of the event (or send this document beforehand):

### The Mentor Brief

**Your role:** Help teams get unstuck without doing the work for them.

**Guidelines:**
1. **Ask questions first.** "What have you tried so far?" and "What do you think is causing the issue?" before jumping to solutions.
2. **Guide, do not code.** Point them to documentation, explain the concept, sketch the architecture on a whiteboard. Avoid taking the keyboard.
3. **Help them scope.** If a team's idea is too ambitious for the time remaining, help them identify the core feature to focus on.
4. **Encourage, do not judge.** A beginner's first API call is a win. Treat it like one.
5. **Spend 10-15 minutes per team max** during walk-throughs. If a problem needs more time, schedule a follow-up or connect them with a more specialized mentor.
6. **Flag serious issues to organizers.** If a team is falling apart, having interpersonal conflict, or seems distressed, let an organizer know.

**You are NOT expected to:**
- Know every technology stack participants are using
- Be available for the entire event (stick to your assigned shift)
- Solve every problem -- "I don't know, but let me find someone who does" is a great answer

## Mentor Rotation Schedule

### For In-Person Events

Set up a mentor station (a designated table or area) where participants can come for help. Also have mentors do periodic walk-throughs.

**Sample schedule for a 24-hour event:**

| Shift | Time | Mentors On |
|-------|------|------------|
| Shift 1 | 1:00 PM - 5:00 PM | 4-5 mentors (heaviest demand as teams start) |
| Shift 2 | 5:00 PM - 10:00 PM | 3-4 mentors |
| Shift 3 | 10:00 PM - 3:00 AM | 2 mentors (lighter demand, but available) |
| Shift 4 | 3:00 AM - 8:00 AM | 1-2 mentors (on-call via Discord, not required on-site) |
| Shift 5 | 8:00 AM - 12:00 PM | 3-4 mentors (teams finishing and polishing) |

**Tip:** The first 3 hours after hacking begins are the highest-demand period. Front-load your best mentors here.

### For Virtual/Hybrid Events
- Create a dedicated help channel in Discord (not just a general chat)
- Use Discord threads so each team's question stays organized
- Have mentors set their status to "Available" when on shift
- Consider voice channels for screen-sharing debug sessions

## The Support Stack

### Discord/Slack Setup for Participant Support

Create these channels before the event:

| Channel | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| #announcements | Organizer updates only (locked to participants) |
| #general | Open chat |
| #help-general | Technical questions for mentors |
| #help-ml | ML/data science specific questions |
| #help-web | Web dev and frontend questions |
| #help-devops | Deployment, APIs, cloud services |
| #team-finding | Pre-event and day-of team formation |
| #off-topic | Memes, music recommendations, decompression |

### Resource Repository
Pin a message or create a channel with:
- Links to challenge track descriptions
- Starter code repositories (if provided)
- API documentation for sponsor tools
- Free tier signup links (cloud credits, API keys)
- Dataset links (if applicable)
- Submission instructions and deadline

## Common Mentorship Situations

| Situation | How to Handle |
|-----------|--------------|
| "We don't know where to start" | Help them break the project into 3-4 tasks. Ask what they are most excited about and start there. |
| "Our model is not working" | Check the basics: data shape, learning rate, input/output dimensions. 80% of ML bugs are data pipeline issues. |
| "We are arguing about what to build" | Help them find common ground. Suggest they pick the idea that is simplest to demo, not the most ambitious. |
| "We are way behind" | Help them cut scope ruthlessly. A working login screen and one feature beats a broken full application. |
| "We finished early" | Suggest polish: better UI, edge case handling, a compelling demo narrative, documentation. |

## Mentor Appreciation

Mentors are volunteering their time. Make them feel valued:

- Provide food and drinks (same as participants)
- Publicly thank them during opening and closing ceremonies
- Send a personal thank-you email after the event
- Offer to write LinkedIn recommendations
- Invite them back for future events
- Share photos and project outcomes so they can see the impact of their help
