# Hackathons

The Hacksgiving playbook. Three years running, $18K in prize money across three nonprofits, and a format we wish we'd had year one.

Hacksgiving started in November 2023 as a collaboration between MAIC and Dr. Jeremy Kedziora (PieperPower Endowed Chair in AI at MSOE). The thesis was narrow on purpose: a 48-hour AI-for-good hackathon paired with one Milwaukee nonprofit. No toy datasets, no made-up problems — the nonprofit showed up, explained their real bottleneck, and students built something they could keep.

| Year | Partner | Students | Teams | Prize Pool | Venue |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2023 | Next Step Clinic | 40+ | 7 | $6,000 | The Cove, Viets Tower |
| 2024 | Discovery World | 70+ | 10 | $6,000 | Diercks Hall |
| 2025 | Milwaukee Domes Alliance | 50+ | 12 | $6,000 | Diercks Hall |

Year one Ben Paulson's team (Team Skippy) won — teammates Sydney Balboni, Ella Bruce, John Cisler, Caitlyn Grant, Tyge Plater, and Brett Storoe — for a patient-screening chatbot. Year three Brett Storoe, now MAIC president, judged alongside the Domes' CEO.

Three years in, we know the shape of a good Hacksgiving: one nonprofit partner, one challenge statement, 48 hours in-person, a $6K pool split 3/2/1, a 4-judge panel that includes the nonprofit's leadership, and a real code handoff where the winning project gets used.

---

## Plan it

**10 weeks of prep. The nonprofit is locked first, the sponsors next, the logistics last.**

### How we did it at MAIC

Dr. Kedziora's Endowed Chair funds the $6K Hacksgiving prize pool directly — we don't shop sponsors for the base event. The 10-week countdown starts with the nonprofit partner. In 2024 we emailed Discovery World's engagement director in early September and had the challenge statement locked by late September. In 2023 we didn't give Next Step Clinic enough prep time before kickoff — they had to explain their workflow twice because we hadn't sent them the problem brief in advance. We fixed that in year two.

By week -6, we draft the problem statement with the nonprofit and share it back for sign-off. By week -4, registration opens on Microsoft Forms. By week -2, we've confirmed food vendors, checked Diercks Hall's A/V, and briefed the judges.

### The repeatable version

**The 10-week countdown:**

| Week out | Milestone |
|---|---|
| -10 | Lock nonprofit partner. Verify they can commit 2 people for judging + intro. |
| -8 | Share draft problem statement with partner. Iterate over 1–2 weeks. |
| -6 | Problem statement finalized. Venue booked. Judges identified. |
| -4 | Registration opens. Promotion starts (Teams, in-class, Discord, social). |
| -2 | Food + catering locked. A/V checked. Judges briefed. |
| -1 | Pre-hackathon Q&A session for registered teams. |
| 0 | Hacksgiving weekend. |

**Lock the partner 10 weeks out.** You want enough time for them to digest what a hackathon is, enough time for you to draft a good problem statement, and enough buffer that weather, illness, or schedule conflicts don't derail you.

**Draft the problem statement together.** Don't write it and send it. Set up a 45-minute call, whiteboard what the nonprofit actually wants, and write it as a collaborative doc. The best problems are *specific enough to scope a 48-hour build* and *general enough that multiple approaches can win.*

**Registration on Microsoft Forms** (or Google Forms). Ask for:
- Name, email, school, year
- Whether they have a team or need one
- Tech skill self-rating (beginner / intermediate / advanced)
- Dietary restrictions

**Team formation:** people with teams register together. People without teams get matched at the kickoff. We cap teams at 7, minimum 2.

**Venue note:** Diercks Hall at MSOE seats ~150. The Cove in Viets Tower holds ~80. Pick your venue by expected registration, not hope.

### What we'd do differently

In 2023 we didn't send Next Step Clinic a draft problem statement before the kickoff — they had to explain their workflow live, which ate 20 minutes of student time. Share your draft problem statement with the partner by week -6. Their domain expertise is the most undervalued input into a good hackathon.

## Run it

**48 hours on the clock. Food, mentorship, judging, and keeping teams alive without keeping them up all night.**

### How we did it at MAIC

Hacksgiving runs Friday night kickoff → Sunday afternoon presentations. Kickoff opens with a 15-minute talk from the nonprofit explaining their mission and the problem, then MAIC walks through the baseline code (if any), the Discord/Teams channel, and the judging criteria. Teams start building at 7pm Friday.

Food rotation: dinner chicken Friday, pizza Saturday lunch, snacks constantly. We do a 2am check-in Saturday night — not to wake anyone up, but so the eboard member on shift can help any team still grinding. We have a "stuck team" rescue protocol Adam Haile refined in 2024: if a team hasn't pushed code in 4 hours, an eboard member checks in with them.

Judging is Sunday afternoon. 3-minute demos + 2-minute Q&A per team, four-judge panel (2 from the nonprofit, 1 MAIC eboard, 1 MSOE faculty). Year one our demos ran 5 minutes each and the whole session went 30 minutes long — we cut to 3 minutes in year two.

### The repeatable version

**Kickoff agenda (90 minutes max):**

1. Welcome + housekeeping (5 min)
2. Nonprofit leadership talk — mission + problem statement (15 min)
3. Technical walkthrough — baseline code, Discord, submission process (15 min)
4. Judging criteria + prize structure (5 min)
5. Team match for solo registrants (30 min)
6. Teams start building (remaining time)

**Food rotation for 50 students:**
- Friday 7pm dinner: chicken + sides
- Saturday 8am breakfast: bagels + coffee
- Saturday 12pm lunch: pizza
- Saturday 6pm dinner: sandwiches
- Sunday 10am brunch: bagels + coffee
- Snacks + drinks continuous

Budget $15/person/day. For 50 students over 2 days that's ~$1,500.

**Mentorship protocol:**
- Eboard members in 6-hour shifts Saturday
- Discord channel for mentor requests
- If a team hasn't pushed code in 4 hours, eboard checks in
- Mentors never write code for teams — they unblock thinking

**Judging:**
- Four judges: 2 from nonprofit (including leadership), 1 MAIC eboard, 1 faculty
- 3-minute demos, 2-minute Q&A
- Rubric: Technical quality 40% / Applicability 40% / Presentation 20%
- Announce top 3 within 30 minutes of the last demo

### What we'd do differently

Year one our demos ran 5 minutes and judging took 3 hours. We cut to 3-minute demos + 2-minute Q&A in year two and the quality of both the demos and the judging went up — students prepped tighter, judges stayed sharper. Time-box demos aggressively.

## Wrap it

**The code handoff to the nonprofit is the actual deliverable. Everything after the winners are announced is what makes the hackathon mean something.**

### How we did it at MAIC

Sunday afternoon, immediately after winners are announced, we hand top-3 teams a shared Google Doc with the nonprofit's primary contact and a 2-week timeline to clean up their code for handoff. Teams that take this seriously get a follow-up recommendation letter from MAIC. Teams that don't, don't.

Within 48 hours we send thank-you emails to every sponsor, every judge, every nonprofit leader who attended, with photos and attendance numbers. The Tuesday after we run a 30-minute eboard retrospective: what worked, what didn't, what to change for next year. We write it up in `Meeting Notes/HacksGiving/` so next year's organizers have context.

The handoff is where Hacksgiving stops being a stunt and starts being useful. In 2023, Team Skippy's chatbot was adopted by Next Step Clinic for their patient intake process. That's why sponsors like Brady Corp and NVIDIA take us seriously — our events produce things.

### The repeatable version

**Within 2 hours of demos ending:**
- Announce winners + distribute prizes (Venmo / PayPal / check)
- Take a group photo for social media
- Hand top-3 teams the nonprofit's contact + 2-week clean-up timeline

**Within 48 hours:**
- Thank-you email to every sponsor (photos, attendance, top-3 summary)
- Thank-you email to every judge
- Thank-you email to the nonprofit (ask: "want to do this again next year?")
- Social media recap post (photos, winners, nonprofit shoutout)

**Within 2 weeks:**
- Top-3 teams deliver cleaned-up code to the nonprofit
- Recommendation letters for teams that delivered
- Retrospective meeting with eboard (30 min, written notes)

**Within 30 days:**
- Pitch your university communications office for a news story
- Reach out to this year's sponsors about next year's event (plant the seed while the impression is warm)
- Update the Hacksgiving `Meeting Notes` doc with everything you learned

### What we'd do differently

For the first year we didn't document the Tuesday retrospective, so when year-two planning kicked off we had to rebuild context from Slack threads. Now we write a one-page retro doc the Tuesday after every Hacksgiving — problems hit, solutions tried, budget actuals, attendance breakdowns. Future organizers inherit a real archive, not tribal knowledge.
