# Challenges and Judging

Good challenge design inspires creative projects. Good judging rewards genuine effort and skill. Get both right and participants leave feeling like the competition was fair, even if they did not win.

## Designing Challenge Tracks

### What Makes a Good Challenge
- **Broad enough** that multiple approaches are valid (not "build exactly this")
- **Specific enough** that participants have a clear starting point (not "do something with AI")
- **Scoped to the time limit** -- a 12-hour hackathon challenge should be achievable by a beginner team in that window
- **Connected to real problems** -- challenges based on real-world needs produce better projects

### Example Challenge Structures

**Single-track (simplest):**
> "Build an AI-powered tool that helps students manage their academic life."

**Multi-track (recommended for 50+ participants):**
- Track 1: AI for Accessibility -- build something that makes technology more inclusive
- Track 2: Data-Driven Campus -- use campus or public data to solve a student problem
- Track 3: Open Innovation -- build anything with an AI/ML component
- Sponsor Track: [Sponsor name] Challenge -- build using [sponsor's API/platform]

**Tip:** Always include an open track. Some of the best hackathon projects do not fit neatly into themed categories.

### Writing Challenge Descriptions

For each track, provide:
1. A one-paragraph description of the problem space
2. 2-3 example project ideas (to spark thinking, not limit it)
3. Any required technologies or datasets (if applicable)
4. Links to relevant APIs, documentation, or starter code
5. The judging criteria specific to that track (if different from general criteria)

## Building a Judging Rubric

### Core Criteria (Works for Most Hackathons)

| Criterion | Weight | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Technical Implementation | 30% | Does it work? How technically sophisticated is the solution? |
| Innovation and Creativity | 25% | Is the idea novel? Does it approach the problem in an interesting way? |
| Impact and Usefulness | 20% | Would someone actually use this? Does it solve a real problem? |
| Presentation and Demo | 15% | Can the team clearly explain what they built and why? |
| Design and User Experience | 10% | Is it usable? Does the interface make sense? |

**Tip:** Share the rubric with participants at the start of the hackathon. Teams that know how they will be judged build better projects.

### Scoring Scale
Use a 1-5 scale for each criterion:
- 1 = Not demonstrated
- 2 = Basic attempt
- 3 = Solid execution
- 4 = Impressive work
- 5 = Exceptional, would not expect this at a student hackathon

Provide judges with a scorecard (Google Sheet or printed form) with the rubric and space for brief notes on each team.

## Recruiting Judges

### Who Makes a Good Judge
- Industry professionals (especially from sponsor companies)
- Faculty with relevant expertise
- Graduate students working in AI/ML
- Alumni who have participated in hackathons
- Fellow club leaders from partner organizations

### How Many Judges
- 15-30 participants: 3 judges
- 50-100 participants: 5 judges
- 150+ participants: 7-9 judges (use a preliminary round)

### Judge Recruitment Timeline
- **6 weeks out:** Reach out to potential judges
- **4 weeks out:** Confirm all judges, send them event details
- **1 week out:** Send judges the rubric, schedule, and logistics brief
- **Day of:** Brief judges in person 30 minutes before demos begin

## Structuring Demo Presentations

### The Biggest Mistake
Letting every team present to everyone for 5 minutes each. With 20 teams, that is nearly 2 hours of presentations. Energy dies. Attention dies. Nobody wins.

### Better Approaches

**Science Fair Style (Best for 15+ teams):**
- Teams set up at tables/stations
- Judges rotate through stations, spending 5 minutes per team
- Each team gives their pitch 3-5 times to different judges
- Top 3-5 teams present to everyone during the closing ceremony
- **Time:** ~45 minutes for judging rotation, 20 minutes for finals

**Two-Round System (Best for 20+ teams):**
- Round 1: Teams present to a single judge or pair of judges (3 minutes each)
- Top 5-8 teams advance to Round 2
- Round 2: Finalists present to all judges and the audience (5 minutes each)
- **Time:** ~60 minutes for Round 1, 30-40 minutes for Round 2

### Presentation Guidelines for Teams
Share this with participants:
- You have [X] minutes. Practice your demo to fit within time.
- Start with the problem, then show the solution. Do not start with "we used TensorFlow..."
- Show a live demo if possible. If the demo might break, have a backup video.
- Every team member should speak at least once.
- End with what you would build next if you had more time.

## Prizes

### Prize Ideas by Budget

**Low budget ($50-150):**
- Gift cards ($25-50 per winning team member)
- Tech accessories (portable chargers, nice cables)
- Books on AI/ML/programming
- Free credits for cloud platforms or APIs

**Mid budget ($200-500):**
- Mechanical keyboards
- Raspberry Pi kits
- Conference tickets
- Larger gift cards ($50-100 per person)

**High budget ($500+):**
- Monitors, headphones, or tablets
- Internship fast-tracks from sponsor companies (most valuable prize you can offer)
- Cash prizes

### Categories Worth Awarding
- Best Overall (1st, 2nd, 3rd)
- Best per challenge track
- Best Beginner Project (huge for encouraging first-timers)
- Most Creative / Most Ambitious
- Best Design
- Audience Choice (let participants vote)

**Tip:** More categories means more winners. At a 50-person hackathon, having 8-10 winning teams means nearly half the room gets recognized. That is good for morale and retention.

## Judging Day-Of Checklist

- [ ] Judges arrived and briefed (30 min before demos)
- [ ] Scorecards printed or digital sheets shared
- [ ] Demo schedule posted and shared with all teams
- [ ] Timer assigned (someone to keep presentations on track)
- [ ] Tally system ready for final scoring
- [ ] Prizes organized and ready to distribute
- [ ] Tie-breaking procedure decided in advance
