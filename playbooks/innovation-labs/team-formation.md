# Team Formation and Kickoff

How you form teams and launch the Lab sets the trajectory for the entire competition. This page covers team composition, the sign-up process, the kickoff event, and how to set expectations.

## The Sign-Up Process

Open sign-ups 2 weeks before kickoff. Collect the following from each participant:

### Sign-Up Survey Fields
- Name and contact info (email, Discord/Slack handle)
- Year in school and major
- Self-rated experience level (beginner / intermediate / advanced) in:
  - Python programming
  - Machine learning
  - Data analysis and visualization
  - The specific domain of the Lab problem (if relevant)
- Preferred role (modeling, data engineering, visualization, project management)
- Weekly hours available during the Lab period
- Any teammates they want to be grouped with (max 1 request)

Keep the survey short. You can collect more detail at kickoff.

## Team Composition Strategy

The goal is balanced teams where every member can contribute and learn.

### The Mix That Works
Each team of 4 should ideally have:
- **1 experienced member** who can unblock technical issues and guide architecture decisions
- **2 intermediate members** who can write code and run experiments with some guidance
- **1 beginner** who is eager to learn and can contribute to data prep, documentation, and presentation

### Why Not Let Students Pick Their Own Teams?
Self-selected teams tend to cluster by skill level. You end up with one "super team" that dominates and several teams that struggle. Intentional team formation produces better outcomes for everyone and more interesting final results.

### How to Assign Teams
1. Sort sign-ups by experience level (use their self-ratings plus any knowledge you have of members)
2. Create a "draft" where you distribute experienced members first, one per team
3. Fill in intermediate members, spreading domain expertise and preferred roles
4. Add beginners, honoring teammate requests where possible
5. Review for balance and adjust

### Handling Teammate Requests
Honor one request per person if possible, but do not let it override balance. If two advanced students want to team up, explain the reasoning and offer to place them on adjacent teams so they can still collaborate informally.

## The Kickoff Event

The kickoff is the single most important event of the Lab. It sets energy, expectations, and momentum.

### Kickoff Agenda (2 hours)

| Time | Activity | Who |
|------|----------|-----|
| 0:00-0:15 | Welcome and Lab overview | Hub leader |
| 0:15-0:35 | Sponsor presentation: company intro and problem reveal | Sponsor representative |
| 0:35-0:45 | Q&A with sponsor | All participants |
| 0:45-0:55 | Team announcements and initial team meetup | Hub leader |
| 0:55-1:15 | Data access walkthrough and technical setup | Hub leader or sponsor engineer |
| 1:15-1:45 | Team working time: review data, assign roles, plan first week | Teams |
| 1:45-2:00 | Wrap-up: timeline review, communication channels, next steps | Hub leader |

### Kickoff Must-Haves
- [ ] Problem brief printed or shared digitally with every participant
- [ ] Data access confirmed and working (test it the day before)
- [ ] Communication channel set up (Slack channel, Discord server, or Teams group)
- [ ] Shared repository created for each team (GitHub org or shared drive)
- [ ] Calendar invites sent for all check-ins, office hours, and showcase
- [ ] Sponsor mentor contact info distributed

## Setting Expectations

Be explicit about what is expected. Distribute these expectations at kickoff:

### For Team Members
- Commit 5-10 hours per week for the duration of the Lab
- Attend all milestone check-ins (or send a representative)
- Communicate with your team at least every 2 days
- Submit all milestones on time
- Treat sponsor data and problem details as confidential unless told otherwise
- Participate in the final showcase regardless of results

### For Team Leads
Each team should designate a team lead at kickoff. The lead is responsible for:
- Scheduling team working sessions
- Submitting milestone deliverables
- Being the primary point of contact with organizers
- Ensuring all team members are contributing
- Flagging problems early (do not wait until Week 3 to say someone dropped off)

### For Sponsors
Set expectations with sponsors before kickoff:
- Respond to student questions within 24 hours during the Lab
- Attend at least 2 office hour sessions
- Attend kickoff and showcase
- Provide feedback on milestone submissions (brief is fine, even a few sentences helps)

## Role Suggestions for Teams

Teams work best when members have clear responsibilities. Suggest these roles at kickoff:

| Role | Responsibilities |
|------|-----------------|
| **Team Lead** | Coordination, timeline management, milestone submissions |
| **Data Engineer** | Data cleaning, pipeline building, feature engineering |
| **Model Developer** | Model selection, training, hyperparameter tuning |
| **Analyst / Presenter** | Evaluation, visualization, error analysis, slide creation |

Roles are not rigid. Everyone should understand the full pipeline. But having a primary owner for each area prevents the "I thought you were doing that" problem.

## First Week Checklist for Teams

Hand this to every team at kickoff:

- [ ] Exchange contact info and set up a team group chat
- [ ] Schedule at least 2 working sessions for Week 1
- [ ] Read the problem brief thoroughly (every member, not just the lead)
- [ ] Load the data and verify access works for all members
- [ ] Run initial exploratory data analysis
- [ ] Write your own problem statement in one paragraph
- [ ] List 3 questions for the sponsor by end of Week 1
- [ ] Prepare Milestone 1 deliverables (due end of Week 1)
