# Finding and Pitching Sponsors

A strong sponsor relationship is the foundation of every Innovation Lab. This page covers how to identify the right companies, craft a pitch that resonates, and structure a partnership that works for both sides.

## Where to Look for Sponsors

Start close and expand outward:

### Tier 1: Warm Connections
- **Career fairs**: Companies already recruiting at your school have budget allocated for student engagement
- **Alumni network**: Graduates working in data science, ML engineering, or product roles at local companies
- **Faculty contacts**: Professors with industry collaborations or consulting relationships
- **Existing club sponsors**: If your club has any corporate sponsors, they are the easiest first conversation
- **Campus employer partners**: Companies that run co-ops or internships with your university

### Tier 2: Local Industry
- **Regional tech companies**: Search LinkedIn for companies within 50 miles that have data science teams
- **Healthcare systems, insurance companies, and manufacturers**: These industries have large datasets and real AI use cases but often struggle to find talent
- **Startups**: Smaller companies may not have cash for big sponsorships but can offer compelling problems and close mentorship

### Tier 3: National and Remote
- **Tech company university programs**: Microsoft, Google, and AWS all have programs for student organizations
- **Industry groups**: Local chambers of commerce, tech councils, or AI-specific meetup groups
- **Conference connections**: If members attend conferences, the companies they meet there are warm leads

## What Sponsors Actually Want

Understanding sponsor motivations helps you pitch effectively. Most sponsors care about some combination of:

1. **Talent pipeline**: Meeting students who might be future hires (this is the primary motivator for most)
2. **Fresh perspectives**: Students approach problems differently than internal teams
3. **Proof of concept**: Getting a low-risk exploration of an AI approach they have been considering
4. **Brand visibility**: Being seen as an innovative, student-friendly employer
5. **Tax-deductible donation**: If your club operates under a university foundation or 501(c)(3)

Do not lead with "we need money." Lead with what you can deliver for them.

## The Pitch

### What to Include

Your initial pitch (email or meeting) should cover:

- **Who you are**: Club name, university, member count, and one line about your track record
- **What an Innovation Lab is**: A multi-week structured competition where student teams solve a real problem using AI
- **What you need from them**: A problem statement, access to data (can be anonymized or synthetic), 2-3 mentors for office hours, and sponsorship funding
- **What they get**: Direct access to 15-40 motivated students, final presentations with actionable insights, first look at top talent, and brand visibility at your university
- **Time commitment**: 10-20 hours total across the entire Lab (problem scoping meeting, kickoff attendance, 2-3 office hours, showcase attendance)

### Sponsorship Tiers

Offer flexibility. Not every company has the same budget.

| Tier | Investment | What They Get |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| **Problem Partner** | $0 (in-kind) | Provides the problem and mentors. No cash, but gets full access to results and talent. |
| **Bronze** | $500-1,000 | Logo on materials, access to team presentations, resume book of participants |
| **Silver** | $1,000-3,000 | Everything above + dedicated problem track, 10-min recruiting slot at showcase |
| **Gold** | $3,000-5,000 | Everything above + naming rights, priority mentor access, featured in all promotion |

Adjust these numbers for your region. $500 goes further at a small state school than at a major metro university.

### Sample Outreach Email

```
Subject: AI Innovation Lab Partnership - [Your University] [Club Name]

Hi [Name],

I lead [Club Name] at [University], a student AI club with [X] active members.
We run Innovation Labs where student teams spend 4-5 weeks solving real industry
problems with AI.

I am reaching out because [specific reason: you saw their job posting for ML
engineers / a mutual contact suggested them / you use their product and see an
AI opportunity].

What we would need from [Company]:
- A problem statement with associated data (can be anonymized)
- 2-3 mentors available for weekly office hours (1 hour each)
- Attendance at the kickoff and final showcase

What you get:
- 4-8 teams of motivated students tackling your problem for a month
- Final presentations with code, models, and recommendations
- Direct access to top student talent before they hit the job market

Our last Lab had [X] teams and [brief result]. Would you be open to a
20-minute call to explore this?

Best,
[Your name]
```

## The Scoping Meeting

Once a sponsor says yes, schedule a 60-minute scoping meeting. The goal is to leave with:

- [ ] A clear problem statement (one paragraph)
- [ ] Understanding of what data is available and how it will be shared
- [ ] Names and contact info for 2-3 technical mentors
- [ ] Agreement on timeline and key dates
- [ ] Sponsorship amount and payment logistics (if applicable)
- [ ] Confirmation of who will attend kickoff and showcase

Bring your problem scoping template (see Templates and Resources) to this meeting.

## Red Flags to Watch For

- **"We will figure out the data later"**: No data by kickoff means a failed Lab. Get a firm commitment.
- **No technical mentors available**: Without mentors, students hit walls they cannot get past. This is non-negotiable.
- **NDA requirements that block student portfolios**: Students need to be able to show their work. Negotiate for anonymized results or a delay window instead of a blanket NDA.
- **Scope creep after agreement**: The sponsor keeps adding requirements after scoping. Pin down the problem statement in writing before kickoff.
- **Single point of contact who is not technical**: You need someone who can answer "what does this column in the dataset mean?" within 24 hours.

## Building Long-Term Partnerships

The best sponsor relationships span multiple semesters:

1. **Debrief after every Lab**: Send a summary report within one week. Include participation numbers, team results, and student feedback about the sponsor experience.
2. **Share hiring outcomes**: If students get interviews or offers through the Lab, tell the sponsor. This is the metric they care about most.
3. **Invite them to other events**: Guest lectures, workshops, and social events keep the relationship warm between Labs.
4. **Increase scope gradually**: First Lab might be one problem with 4 teams. Second Lab could be two problems with 8 teams. Let it grow naturally.
5. **Thank them publicly**: Social media posts, newsletter mentions, and a spot on your website go a long way.
