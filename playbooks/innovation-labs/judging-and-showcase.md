# Judging and Showcase

The showcase is the culmination of weeks of work. A well-run showcase leaves students proud, sponsors impressed, and the audience excited about AI. This page covers presentation structure, judging rubrics, event logistics, and how to handle awards.

## Presentation Format

Each team gets a fixed slot. The standard format is:

- **8 minutes for the presentation** (strict cutoff)
- **5 minutes for Q&A** from judges
- **2 minutes for transition** between teams

With 8 teams, that is roughly 2 hours. Add 15 minutes for opening remarks and 15 minutes for deliberation and awards. Total event: about 2.5 hours.

### Recommended Slide Structure (8-10 slides)

| Slide | Content | Time |
|-------|---------|------|
| 1 | Title, team name, team members | 15 sec |
| 2 | Problem statement in the team's own words | 45 sec |
| 3 | Data overview and key observations from EDA | 1 min |
| 4 | Approach: what methods they tried and why | 1.5 min |
| 5-6 | Results: metrics, comparisons, visualizations | 2 min |
| 7 | Error analysis: where the model fails and insights | 1 min |
| 8 | Business recommendations: what the sponsor should do with this | 1 min |
| 9 | Lessons learned and next steps | 30 sec |
| 10 | Questions slide with team contact info | - |

### Presentation Tips to Share with Teams
- Lead with the business problem, not the technical approach
- Show one compelling visualization rather than five mediocre ones
- Explain model choices in plain language (judges may include non-technical sponsor leaders)
- Practice the timing. Teams always run over in rehearsal.
- Every team member should speak during the presentation

## The Judging Rubric

A clear rubric ensures fair evaluation and gives teams actionable feedback. Share the rubric with teams at kickoff so they know what to optimize for.

### Scoring Categories

| Category | Weight | Description |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| **Problem Understanding** | 15% | Did the team clearly understand and articulate the business problem? |
| **Technical Approach** | 25% | Was the methodology sound? Did they try multiple approaches? Is the code reproducible? |
| **Results and Analysis** | 25% | How well did the model perform? Did they do meaningful error analysis? |
| **Business Impact** | 20% | Are the recommendations actionable? Did they connect technical results to business value? |
| **Presentation Quality** | 15% | Was the presentation clear, well-organized, and within time? |

### Scoring Scale
Use a 1-5 scale for each category:
- **5**: Exceptional. Exceeds expectations for undergraduate work.
- **4**: Strong. Clear competence and thoughtful execution.
- **3**: Adequate. Meets basic expectations but has gaps.
- **2**: Below expectations. Significant issues in approach or execution.
- **1**: Incomplete. Major components missing.

## Selecting Judges

Aim for 3-5 judges. A good panel includes:

- **1-2 sponsor representatives** (ideally the mentors plus a senior leader)
- **1 faculty member** with AI/ML expertise
- **1 industry professional** outside the sponsor company (for objectivity)
- **1 experienced student** (optional: a club alumnus or graduate student)

### Briefing Judges
Send judges the following at least 3 days before the showcase:
- The problem brief
- The judging rubric with scoring guide
- The presentation schedule and team names
- Logistics (parking, room location, wifi access)
- Reminder that written feedback for each team is expected (even brief notes matter to students)

## Showcase Event Logistics

### Before the Event
- [ ] Room booked with projector and screen tested
- [ ] Microphone available (essential for 30+ attendees)
- [ ] Presentation laptop ready with all slide decks loaded (or have teams submit slides 24 hours before)
- [ ] Timer visible to presenters (phone timer on a stand works fine)
- [ ] Food and drinks ordered (budget $3-5 per attendee)
- [ ] Judge score sheets printed
- [ ] Name tags for judges and sponsor guests
- [ ] Photographer or designated person to take photos

### Day-of Timeline

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| -30 min | Room setup, AV check, food arranged |
| -15 min | Judges arrive, receive score sheets and briefing |
| 0:00 | Welcome remarks (hub leader) |
| 0:05 | Sponsor opening words (2 minutes) |
| 0:07 | Team presentations begin |
| ~1:50 | All presentations complete, judges begin deliberation |
| ~2:05 | Judges return, awards announced |
| 2:15 | Networking and informal conversations |
| 2:45 | Event ends |

### Running the Presentations
- Assign a timekeeper who gives signals at 6 minutes (2 min warning) and 8 minutes (time)
- Cut teams off at 8 minutes politely but firmly. Fairness matters.
- Let Q&A go slightly over if judges are asking good questions, but keep it under 7 minutes total
- Have a "transition slide" between teams with the next team name and a 60-second countdown

## Awards and Recognition

### Award Categories
At minimum:
- **1st Place**: Best overall team
- **2nd Place**: Runner-up
- **Best Technical Approach**: Strongest methodology (may not be the top performer)

Optional additional awards:
- **Best Presentation**: Clearest communication
- **Most Creative Approach**: Most innovative or unexpected methodology
- **Best Error Analysis**: Deepest understanding of model limitations
- **People's Choice**: Audience vote (use a quick hand-raise or phone poll)

### Prizes
Keep prizes meaningful but proportionate to the sponsorship budget:
- **1st Place**: $200-500 per team member (gift cards or cash) or equivalent in tech gear
- **2nd Place**: $100-250 per team member
- **Category awards**: $50-100 per team member or a meaningful non-cash prize (books, conference tickets, sponsor swag)

If budget is tight, focus on recognition over cash. A LinkedIn recommendation from the sponsor's VP of Engineering is worth more than a $50 gift card.

### What Every Team Should Get
Regardless of results, every participating team should receive:
- Written feedback from judges (even 3-4 sentences per category)
- A certificate of participation (sounds small, but students put these on resumes)
- Public recognition at the event and in follow-up communications
- Permission to use their project in their portfolio (confirm with sponsor)

## After the Showcase

The event is over, but do not stop yet:

1. **Same day**: Send a thank-you message to all participants, judges, and sponsors
2. **Within 48 hours**: Share photos and highlights on social media and your club newsletter
3. **Within 1 week**: Distribute judge feedback to each team individually
4. **Within 1 week**: Send sponsor the debrief summary (see Post-Lab Follow-Through)
5. **Within 2 weeks**: Collect student feedback via a post-Lab survey
